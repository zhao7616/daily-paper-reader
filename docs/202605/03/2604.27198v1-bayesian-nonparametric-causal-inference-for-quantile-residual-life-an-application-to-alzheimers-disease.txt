ABSTRACT 

In Alzheimer’s disease research, for individuals who remain dementia-free through a given follow-up time, an important clinical question is how much longer they are likely to remain dementia-free. Quantiles of this remaining time provide clinically interpretable prognostic milestones and can help characterize prognostic heterogene-ity across baseline groups. We address this question in the Alzheimer’s Disease Neuroimaging Initiative (ADNI), focusing on baseline amyloid status as the ex-

Estimation is challenging because amyloid status is observed rather than randomized, requiring adjustment for confounding, and because time to dementia onset is heterogeneous and heavily right-censored. We estimate causal contrasts in quantile residual life using a Bayesian nonparametric enriched Dirichlet process mixture model for the joint distribution of event times, exposure, and baseline co-variates, with inference via Bayesian g-computation. The approach accommodates ignorable missing baseline covariates through data augmentation, supports inference across clinically relevant landmark times, and allows sensitivity analysis for residual unmeasured confounding. Simulation studies show good performance under com-plex heterogeneity and heavy censoring. In ADNI, elevated baseline amyloid was associated with shorter quantiles of remaining dementia-free time than non-elevated baseline amyloid among individuals who remained dementia-free through relevant landmark times, overall and within baseline diagnostic subgroups.

1 INTRODUCTION 

Alzheimer’s disease (AD) research increasingly calls for clinically interpretable summaries of prognosis in real-world populations. In particular, for individuals who remain dementia-free through a given follow-up time, a clinically important question is how much longer they are likely to remain dementia-free and whether that remaining time differs across biologically meaningful baseline groups. This question is especially relevant in the Alzheimer’s Disease Neuroimaging Initiative (ADNI), where baseline amyloid status is strongly associated with disease progression and has taken on added clinical importance following recent U.S. Food and Drug Administration approvals of amyloid beta–directed monoclonal antibodies for early AD in patients with confirmed amyloid pathology (U.S. Food and Drug Administration 2023, 2024). Our scientific goal is therefore to compare remaining time to dementia onset between elevated and non-elevated baseline amyloid groups among individuals who have remained dementia-free through a given follow-up time, Page 2 of 53 denoted by the landmark time ν (Dafni 2011). Because this target conditions on surviving dementia-free through ν, it is inherently dynamic and differs from conventional survival summaries defined from study entry. 

1.1 ADNI study 

To motivate the proposed framework, we analyze observational survival data from ADNI, a large, longitudinal, multicenter study of AD. ADNI was launched in 2003 as a public–private partnership to evaluate whether neuroimaging measures, other biological markers, and clinical and neuropsycho-logical assessments can be combined to measure progression of mild cognitive impairment (MCI) and early AD (adni.loni.usc.edu). ADNI collects harmonized longitudinal data across multiple clini-cal sites, including clinical and cognitive assessments, neuroimaging measures, genetic information, and fluid biomarkers. This setting is especially well suited to our problem because it combines rich baseline characterization with follow-up for dementia onset and reflects the confounding, heterogeneity, and heavy right censoring common in real-world observational studies. Our scientific objective is to compare remaining time to dementia onset between elevated and non-elevated baseline amyloid groups among individuals who remain event-free through a given follow-up time, both overall and within baseline diagnostic subgroups. We place particular emphasis on individuals with MCI, for whom progression to dementia is common over short- to medium-term follow-up, yielding estimates that are both clinically relevant and statistically stable. We also report results for cognitively normal (CN) participants but treat those analyses as exploratory because dementia events are comparatively rare over the available follow-up and conditioning on landmark survival further reduces the effective sample size. Because amyloid status is observational rather than randomized in ADNI, individuals with elevated and non-elevated amyloid may differ systematically in demographic, genetic and clinical characteristics that are also associated with subsequent disease progression. As a result, contrasts in prognosis across baseline amyloid groups require careful adjustment for confounding. Exploratory summaries of the observed follow-up data are presented in Figure 1. 1.2 Existing approaches 

For a dynamic prognosis, a more directly interpretable target is the quantile residual life (QRL) function, defined as the ρ-quantile of the remaining lifetime among individuals who survive beyond 

ν (Fligner & Rust 1982, Jeong et al. 2008). Unlike mean-based models, QRL is less sensitive to extreme outcomes in heavy-tailed settings and directly addresses when a specified proportion of survivors are expected to experience the event, making it useful for defining prognostic windows in clinically relevant subgroups (Jung et al. 2009, Kim et al. 2012, Jeong 2014, Lin et al. 2019). Despite these advantages, causal inference for QRL in observational studies remains underdeveloped, especially in the presence of heavy censoring and heterogeneous disease progression. Recent work by Hong et al. (2026) introduces a frequentist causal framework for QRL through the observable survivor quantile contrast (OSQC). While that framework provides a principled causal estimand, its practical implementation can be challenging in settings such as ADNI. In particular, inverse probability censoring weighting may be sensitive to model misspecification, unstable in finite samples and inefficient when pre-landmark information is not fully utilized. More broadly, the combination of an observational exposure, heavy right censoring, and heterogeneous disease progression complicates estimation of dynamic causal contrasts in remaining time to onset of dementia. A flexible Bayesian nonparametric (BNP) approach is therefore attractive in this setting, but standard Dirichlet process mixture models (DPMMs) may perform poorly when high-dimensional covariates dominate clustering and degrade conditional outcome estimation (Park et al. 2012, Roy et al. 2018, Bae et al. 2024). The methodological challenge is to develop an approach that preserves modeling flexibility while remaining well suited for causal estimation of QRL under confounding, heterogeneity, and censoring. 

1.3 Our approach 

To address these challenges, we study the OSQC within a Bayesian causal framework for observa-tional survival data. We treat baseline amyloid status as a clinically defined exposure and estimate Page 5 of 53 the resulting contrasts using an enriched DPMM (EDPMM) for the joint distribution of the observed data. This joint modeling strategy supports posterior g-computation without a separately estimated propensity-score model, accommodates heterogeneous AD progression patterns, and helps avoid the covariate-domination problem that can impair a standard DPMM. The framework also handles substantial right censoring, missing baseline covariates, and inference for multiple pν, ρ q combi-nations from a single posterior fit. We assess its performance through the ADNI application and supporting simulation studies.

In such settings, the covariates can dominate the clustering mechanism under a single-layer DPMM, leading to fragmented outcome partitions and degraded estimation of the conditional outcome distribution (Roy et al. 2018). The EDPMM mitigates this covariate-domination problem by separating the partition into main outcome clusters and nested exposure–covariate subclusters. That hierarchical structure induces covariate-dependent mixture weights Λkpz, xq, allowing relatively simple local kernels to represent complex global dependence among Y , Z, and X. As a result, the model produces the stable conditional outcome distributions needed for reliable g-computation. The joint modeling strategy can also improve finite-sample stability when overlap is limited; see Assumption (A4) . Unlike inverse-probability weighting approaches, which can become numerically unstable in sparse covariate regions or under rare exposure patterns, the EDPMM regularizes estimation by borrowing information across structurally similar profiles. Such regularization can yield smoother conditional survival estimates without ad hoc weight trimming, although it does not replace the need for the underlying positivity assumption. Page 12 of 53 4 POSTERIOR COMPUTATION 

To generate posterior samples from the observed data, we use a Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithm based on an extension of Neal (2000, Algorithm 8) to the EDPMM setting (Wade et al. 2014). Under right censoring, direct implementation is complicated by survival-function contributions in the observed-data likelihood, which break conjugacy of the local outcome kernels. To avoid computationally burdensome Metropolis-within-Gibbs tuning across dynamically evolving nonparametric clusters, we adopt a Bayesian data augmentation strategy in which the unobserved event times for censored individuals ( Di “ 0) are treated as latent variables. The augmentation yields a complete-data representation that is more convenient for posterior computation. It facilitates MCMC updating of the outcome model using augmented event times, while missing baseline covariates are handled through an additional full-conditional imputation step under an ignorable missingness assumption. The flexible EDPMM specification also allows the conditional distribution of the covariates to vary across latent subclusters, which can make the implied missing-at-random assumption more plausible by accommodating local heterogeneity in the joint distribution. Full details of the MCMC algorithm are provided in Section S3 of the supplementary materials, including the data augmentation steps for censoring (Section S3.1), imputation of missing covariates (Section S3.2), and updates for cluster assignments and model parameters (Section S3.3). Posterior inference for the causal estimands is then carried out through Bayesian g-computation applied to the retained MCMC samples. Because the EDPMM specifies the joint distribution of the complete data, each posterior draw induces a corresponding conditional survival function. Marginal causal quantities are obtained by integrating these conditional survival functions over the empirical covariate distribution. In practice, we approximate this integration for each retained posterior sample by Monte Carlo averaging over a synthetic cohort of size M . The predictive weight derivations and the full post-processing algorithm are provided in Section S4 of the supplementary materials. This post-processing strategy provides an important computational advantage. A single posterior fit can be used to obtain posterior summaries, point estimates, and p1 ´ αq level credible intervals for Page 13 of 53 arbitrary pν, ρ q pairs, as well as for other functionals of the survival distribution, without repeated model refitting. 

5 SIMULATION STUDY 

To evaluate the finite-sample performance of the proposed EDPMM approach, we conduct a simulation study comparing it with a standard DPMM alternative. Building on the general survival-mixture framework of Park et al. (2012), we extend the design to include multiple baseline covariates and covariate-dependent exposure assignment, thereby inducing realistic confounding. The data-generating mechanisms considered here feature complex covariate dependence, heavy-tailed and multimodal survival distributions, and exposure effects that vary across mixture components. A comprehensive overview of the simulation design and performance metrics is provided in Section S5, and the full parameter configurations for each scenario are given in Section S5.1. 

5.1 Simulation Setup 

We generate simulated datasets with sample sizes N P t 500 , 1500 u. The data-generating mechanism is designed to incorporate dependence among baseline covariates, covariate-dependent exposure assignment, heterogeneous survival distributions, and covariate-dependent censoring. Specifically, we simulate a five-dimensional baseline covariate vector Xi consisting of two binary and three continuous variables with dependent structure, and generate the binary exposure Zi P t 0, 1u from a covariate-dependent probit model to induce confounding. Conditional on pZ, Xq, the log-survival time is generated from a two-component mixture of a Student’s t distribution with 10 degrees of freedom and a normal distribution, with mixture weights 

0.4 and 0.6, respectively. This specification is motivated in part by the exploratory ADNI summaries in Figure 1b, which suggest substantial skewness and possible multimodality in the observed follow-up distribution. 6 APPLICATION 

We apply the proposed framework to the ADNI cohort to estimate causal contrasts in residual time to dementia onset under elevated versus non-elevated baseline amyloid status. Our primary analysis targets the marginal OSQC in the overall cohort at landmark times ν P t 0, 5, 10 u and quantile levels 

ρ P t 0.1, 0.2, 0.3u, together with subgroup-specific results by baseline diagnostic status. We place particular emphasis on the baseline MCI subgroup, where dementia progression is more frequent over follow-up and prognostic contrasts are especially clinically relevant. Results for the baseline CN subgroup are also reported, but are interpreted more cautiously because event information is sparse and effective sample sizes decrease further at later landmark times; see Section S6. 

6.1 Application Setup 

Given the observational nature of baseline amyloid status, adjustment for baseline confounders is required to support Assumption (A2) . Our adjustment set X includes age, sex, years of education, baseline clinical diagnosis, and APOE ε4 carrier status. There were no missing values in baseline diagnosis, amyloid status, age, or sex in the final analytic sample. We observed 36 missing values for APOE ε4 carrier status and 1 missing value for education; rather than excluding these subjects, we handle the missing covariates through the EDPMM data augmentation procedure under the ignorable missingness assumption described in Section S3.2. The analytic cohort was constructed by linking a subject-level participant roster with a diagnosis dataset used to determine baseline diagnostic status. We began with 5068 subjects in the roster. Of these, 1358 were excluded before merging because no corresponding diagnosis record was available, leaving 3710 subjects in the merged dataset. After applying the analytic eligibility criteria, an additional 2397 subjects were excluded, yielding a final analytic sample of N “ 1313 participants with baseline CN or MCI, available baseline amyloid status, and valid follow-up information. Page 19 of 53 In the final analytic cohort, 533 participants ( 40 .59% ) were elevated amyloid at baseline ( Z “ 1), and 517 (39 .38% ) were APOE ε4 carriers. The mean age was 70 .96 years (SD “ 7.19 ), 53 .39% 

of participants were female, and the mean years of education was 16 .45 (SD “ 2.48 ). During follow-up, 186 subjects ( 14 .17% ) experienced clinical dementia onset and 1127 (85 .83% ) were right censored. Event incidence differed markedly across baseline risk strata: by baseline amyloid status, the event rate was 4.62% (36 {780 ) in the non-elevated amyloid group and 28 .14% (150 {533 ) in the elevated amyloid group, while by baseline diagnosis the event rate was 2.91% (20 {688 ) among CN participants and 26 .56% (166 {625 ) among MCI participants. These patterns highlight substantial heterogeneity in event information across subgroups, with lower precision expected in lower-risk strata and at later follow-up times. Heavy right censoring, sparse events in some subgroups, and landmark conditioning therefore make direct empirical estimation of residual-life contrasts difficult. In this setting, our BNP model borrows strength across covariate profiles to stabilize conditional survival estimation for g-computation, where frequentist approaches may be unstable due to event sparsity and variable effective sample sizes. 7 DISCUSSION 

In the ADNI application, elevated baseline amyloid was consistently associated with shorter remaining time to dementia onset overall and in the baseline MCI subgroup, across landmark times and quantile levels. This pattern was especially clinically informative in the MCI subgroup, where progression was more common and separation in dementia-free survival was most pronounced. These findings indicate that baseline amyloid status carries prognostic information not only for overall dementia risk, but also for remaining dementia-free time among individuals who remain event-free through clinically relevant landmark times. Sensitivity analyses further showed that, although the magnitude of the contrast varied under alternative assumptions, its direction and overall qualitative pattern were preserved. Page 25 of 53 To study this question, we proposed a BNP framework for QRL analysis under a causal landmark perspective. Using an EDPMM for joint modeling of the outcome, exposure, and confounders, the approach supports g-computation without separate nuisance-model estimation, accommodates heterogeneous outcome structure, and alleviates covariate domination. Compared with Hong et al. (2026), it supports inference across multiple pν, ρ q combinations from a single posterior fit, accommodates missing baseline covariates, and avoids inverse-probability weighting instability. Simulation studies showed that the method remained numerically stable and accurately recovered the target contrasts across nonlinear and heterogeneous settings, while the DPMM was more susceptible to degradation from covariate domination. Results under a more conservative nominal level also supported our use of 99% credible intervals in the heavily censored ADNI application. As in any causal analysis of observational data, the proposed framework relies on identifying assumptions that are not fully verifiable from the observed data, particularly conditional exchange-ability and conditionally non-informative censoring; see Assumptions (A2) and (A3) . In the ADNI application, we argued that these assumptions are plausible after adjustment for important baseline demographic, genetic, and clinical covariates, and we supplemented the primary analysis with sensi-tivity analyses for residual unmeasured confounding and informative censoring; see Section 6.2. Although these analyses do not eliminate reliance on the identifying assumptions, they provide a transparent assessment of robustness under clinically interpretable departures from the benchmark causal model. However, the current sensitivity analyses rely on restricted location shift perturbations (for Assumptions (A2) and (A3) ) and require computationally expensive refitting of the posterior sampler for each specification (for Assumption (A3) ). Future work could therefore develop richer and more computationally scalable sensitivity analyses for violations of Assumptions (A2) and (A3) ,including approaches based on more flexible bias models (Wang & Daniels 2011, Linero & Daniels 2015), or joint dependence constructions such as copulas (Daniels et al. 2012, Kim et al. 2017, Bae et al. 2024). A second limitation is that the OSQC, while clinically intuitive, conditions on post-baseline survival and is therefore inherently vulnerable to informative selection, complicating causal interpretation across different survivors. In related work, Hong et al. (2026) considered the principal survivor Page 26 of 53 quantile contrast (PSQC) to address this issue more directly. PSQC targets a different causal question and may be appealing when interest centers on principal strata defined by joint survival status, but it also requires additional principal-strata assumptions that are strong and not empirically verifiable (Frangakis & Rubin 2002, VanderWeele 2011). In principle, the proposed EDPMM framework could be extended to estimate the PSQC once those assumptions are imposed. In ADNI, however, where right censoring is substantial and landmarking further reduces the effective sample size, such an extension may be more sensitive to assumption violations and harder to estimate stably in practice. We therefore focus on the OSQC as a more direct and clinically interpretable estimand for exploratory dynamic prognosis across multiple landmark times ν and quantile levels 

ρ. Extending the proposed EDPMM framework to principal-stratum causal estimands such as the PSQC remains an important direction for future work. Several additional extensions are also of interest. First, scaling the framework to accommodate high-dimensional covariates would broaden its applicability to modern biomedical settings with richer baseline feature information. Second, incorporating multivariate longitudinal biomarkers into the QRL model could support dynamic prediction and causal evaluation of QRL as patient health trajectories evolve over time. More broadly, extending the proposed framework to settings with time-varying exposures or treatment regimes would further expand its usefulness for studying dynamic causal effects on residual lifetime in longitudinal observational studies.

S2.1 Sensitivity Analysis for Unmeasured Confounding

A practical advantage of the proposed framework is that this analysis can be implemented entirely through post-processing of the posterior draws, without refitting the MCMC sampler. S3.1 Data Augmentation for the Censored Outcome

Without conjugacy, one standard alternative is to employ a Metropolis-within-Gibbs algorithm. It requires careful tuning of the proposal distributions to achieve an optimal acceptance rate; poor tuning can lead to highly inefficient sampling, slow exploration of Page 35 of 53 the parameter space, and ultimately, poor mixing and failure to converge. To circumvent these computational bottlenecks, we employ a Bayesian data augmentation strategy. By treating the true, unobserved event times for the censored individuals as latent variables, we can ‘augment’ the data to restore the ‘complete-data’ likelihood. This approach entirely avoids the need for Metropolis-Hastings steps. In standard Metropolis-within-Gibbs algorithms, acceptance rates are typically much lower due to the lack of a universally efficient proposal distribution, necessitating continuous and often difficult tuning. S3.2 Handling Missingness in Covariates 

In observational survival studies, it is common to encounter missingness across various data components. While the joint formulation of the proposed EDPMM framework makes it technically possible to impute completely missing outcomes or exposures, subjects lacking these primary variables are typically excluded from the analysis to prevent unwarranted model extrapolation. Conversely, partially observed event times are naturally handled as right-censored outcomes via the data augmentation procedure described in Section S3.1. Furthermore, to address the frequent occurrence of missingness in the baseline covariates, X, we leverage the joint modeling capabilities of the EDPMM framework. Under an ignorable missingness assumption, incomplete covariate data are seamlessly handled by embedding an additional MCMC data augmentation step within our Gibbs sampler. It is important to clarify the missingness assumption in our mixture modeling framework. We assume that the covariate missingness mechanism is Missing at Random (MAR) within each latent subcluster ; that is, conditional on the current cluster assignment (and its parameters), the missingness indicator depends only on observed quantities and not on the missing covariate values Page 37 of 53 themselves. Because the variables are locally independent given the latent cluster assignment but strongly dependent marginally, this cluster-conditional MAR assumption need not imply marginal MAR in the observed-data distribution. In particular, if the missingness mechanism depends on the latent cluster assignment, then marginally it can be viewed as Missing Not At Random (MNAR), since the cluster assignment depends on the (partially) unobserved data.
