# ABSTRACT 

Whole-aircraft diagnosis for general aviation faces threefold challenges: data uncertainty, task het-erogeneity, and computational inefficiency. Existing end-to-end approaches uniformly model health discrimination and fault characterization, overlooking intrinsic receptive field conflicts between global context modeling and local feature extraction, while incurring prohibitive training costs under severe class imbalance. To address these, this study proposes the Diagnosis Decomposition Framework (DDF), explicitly decoupling diagnosis into Anomaly Detection (AD) and Fault Classification (FC) subtasks via the Long-Micro Scale Diagnostician (LMSD). Employing a "long-range global screening and micro-scale local precise diagnosis" strategy, LMSD utilizes Convolutional Tokenizer with Multi-Head Self-Attention (ConvTokMHSA) for global operational pattern discrimination and Multi-Micro Kernel Network (MMK Net) for local fault feature extraction. Decoupled training separates "large-sample lightweight" and "small-sample complex" optimization pathways, significantly reducing computational overhead. Concurrently, Keyness Extraction Layer (KEL) via knowledge distillation furnishes physically traceable explanations for two-stage decisions, materializing interpretability-by-design. Experiments on the NGAFID real-world aviation dataset demonstrate approximately 4–8% improvement in Multi-Class Weighted Penalty Metric (MCWPM) over baselines with substantially reduced training time, validating comprehensive advantages in task adaptability, interpretability, and efficiency. This provides a deployable methodology for general aviation health management.

Real-world operations, however, exhibit substantial uncertainty—noisy sensor measurements and ambiguous labels pervade operational datasets [ 10 , 11 ]. The NGAFID dataset—comprising Cessna 172 fleet data with 23-dimensional flight profiles [ 8]—exemplifies three inherent challenges: uncertainty (environmental noise and label ambiguity), target component sparsity (fault signatures submerged in low-variance subspaces), and multi-stage coupling (dynamic interdependencies masking local faults). End-to-end training on full datasets incurs prohibitive costs, while augmentation risks distribution bias. Combined with the need for deep nonlinear feature extraction due to sparsity and coupling, these factors render collaborative optimization of heterogeneous subtasks under strict safety constraints a critical unresolved challenge in aviation PHM. In recent years, data-driven fault diagnosis has emerged as a central pillar of PHM, accumulating extensive expertise in feature extraction and pattern recognition across diverse subsystem-level applications [ 20 , 21 , 22 ]—including rotating machinery (bearings and gears) [ 23 , 24 , 25 , 26 ], engines[ 27 , 28 ], and new energy systems such as lithium-ion batteries 2Task Decomposition for Aircraft Health Diagnosis [29 ], fuel cells [ 30 , 31 ], and wind turbines [ 10 , 32 ]. Methodological advancements have undergone a paradigm transition from traditional signal processing techniques (e.g., EEMD, wavelet analysis) [ 23 , 33 ] to deep learning architectures (CNNs [ 24 , 34 ], RNNs [ 34 , 35 ]), and subsequently to attention mechanisms [ 36 , 37 ], graph neural networks [ 38 , 39 ], transfer learning [40, 41, 42], physics-informed hybrid models [43, 44, 45], and foundation models [46, 47]. However, existing research predominantly focuses on idealized scenarios under component-level, closed-set, and balanced-data assumptions [ 48 , 49 ]. When confronted with whole-aircraft level aviation flight parameter data such as NGAFID [8], these approaches face structural challenges in the following three dimensions: 

Insufficient task adaptability: Architectural conflicts. Existing end-to-end approaches conflate health discrimination and fault characterization into monolithic mappings, ignoring their divergent granularity requirements: AD demands full-sequence receptive fields to capture cross-temporal operational contexts [ 1], whereas FC requires restricted fields to isolate local fault features and suppress cross-stage noise [ 17 ]. This receptive field paradox renders single architectures fundamentally compromised—global attention mechanisms introduce excessive noise for fine-grained FC, while local constraints sacrifice critical operational context for AD. Contemporary attempts to reconcile these conflicts within unified architectures, such as cross-scale interactive attention networks for aviation pump diagnosis [ 50 ], merely enforce implicit global-local trade-offs rather than resolving the underlying objective conflict through architectural separation. 

Weak Interpretability: Black-box mechanisms and credibility crises. Modern aviation demands transparency beyond mere performance metrics—requiring demonstrable evidence of how and why models function to ensure flight safety credibility [ 2 ]. However, conventional deep learning models operate as black boxes [ 51 ], while post-hoc interpretability techniques (e.g., SHAP, LIME) [ 11 , 52 ] struggle to integrate with physical mechanisms and fault propagation pathways [ 43 ]. Recent systematic benchmarks reveal that even state-of-the-art uncertainty quantification methods (e.g., Monte Carlo Dropout, Deep Ensembles) for deep learning prognostics exhibit significant overconfidence on out-of-distribution data and fail to provide reliable confidence estimates in safety-critical aviation applications [53 ]. Critically, end-to-end models entangle health discrimination and fault attribution within shared parameter spaces, rendering explanations incapable of disentangling operational heterogeneity from fault signatures—thus failing to answer whether discrimination stems from operational memorization or essential fault features. This necessitates interpretability-by-design approaches that embed physical traceability and transparency through architectural task decoupling [54]. 

Computational efficiency bottlenecks: Deployability constraints. While computational optimization is not aviation PHM's primary objective, training efficiency and inference overhead determine deployability [ 55 ]. Real flight data exhibit extreme class imbalance [ 8 ]: training complex end-to-end models on massive healthy samples incurs prohibitive costs, while augmenting scarce fault samples risks distribution bias. Coupled with strict constraints on model size and latency [ 16 ], existing methods lack mechanisms to decouple data scales—rendering them unable to reconcile training economy with deployment feasibility. This paper addresses task adaptability, interpretability, and computational efficiency in whole-aircraft aviation PHM through three principal contributions: (1) Task Decomposition : The Diagnosis Decomposition Framework (DDF) and its instantiation, the Long-Micro Scale Diagnostician (LMSD), explicitly decouple diagnosis into Anomaly Detection (AD) and Fault Classification (FC). Architecturally separating global operational discrimination by Convolutional Tokenizer with Multi-Head Self-Attention (ConvTokMHSA) with full-sequence receptive fields from local fault extraction by Multi-Micro Kernel Network (MMK Net) with restricted fields resolves the receptive field paradox inherent in end-to-end approaches. (2) Interpretability-by-Design : A two-stage independent interpretability mechanism via the Keyness Extrac-tion Layer (KEL) enhances model credibility. Using knowledge distillation, KEL extracts spatio-temporal attention distributions for both stages, providing physical traceability and distinguishing operational pattern memorization from essential fault features inside profiles. (3) Efficient Training : The decoupled training strategy pairs large-sample lightweight models (AD) with small-sample complex models (FC). Hard-isolated training with frozen-parameter cascading and hard-threshold routing minimizes missed detections while substantially reducing training time, enhancing deployment feasibility. # 2 Data Description 

To investigate health management for general aviation aircraft in real industrial scenarios, this study adopts the NGAFID aviation maintenance dataset as the core analytical subject [ 8 ]. This dataset comprises 23-dimensional sensor time-series data (sampled at 1 Hz) encompassing over 28,000 flights (accumulating 31,000 flight hours) from a Cessna 172 fleet, associated with 36 categories of unscheduled maintenance events. Unlike idealized simulation environments, NGAFID was acquired from real-world diversified operational scenarios. Furthermore, as its sensor network covers critical whole-aircraft subsystems and fault propagation paths manifest multi-system coupling characteristics, it essentially constitutes a whole-airframe level Diagnosis dataset. Based on physical functionality, the 23-dimensional sensors can be partitioned into two complementary observation sets (see Table 1): monitoring data (encompassing electrical, fuel, engine, and cylinder status channels) directly reflects the physical health states of powerplant and avionics systems; operational pattern data (flight status parameters including IAS/VSpd/AltMSL and environmental parameter OAT) characterizes flight mission profiles, piloting styles, and external meteorological conditions. This binary partition reveals the core characteristic of aviation flight parameter data—the normal envelope and anomalous deviations of monitoring data are both nested within specific operational patterns. The strong coupling between these two data types necessitates that health discrimination be performed within operational contexts rather than relying on absolute thresholds. NGAFID comprises the benchmark Subset (19 classes, 11,446 flights) and Overall dataset (36 classes, 28,935 flights), both exhibiting dual imbalance: healthy/anomalous quantitative disparity and extreme long-tailed fault distributions (head classes > 2,000 flights versus < 15 for tails, three orders of magnitude). This reflects objective fleet reality— chronic wear predominates over acute failures—rather than sampling bias. The binary health/anomaly opposition, fine-grained identification demands, and strong operational-monitoring coupling impose structural challenges on end-to-end diagnosis: global noise obscures local signals while imbalance impairs tail-class recall, motivating task decomposition. The NGAFID dataset exhibits the following characteristics: 

Uncertainty. Sourced from Cessna 172 fleet operations at a real-world flight school, NGAFID encompasses data collected across diverse seasons, weather conditions, pilot behaviors, and mission profiles. Unlike simulation datasets (e.g., C-MAPSS), it contains authentic environmental noise and uncertainty; label ambiguity also arises from the complexity of actual maintenance records. # 3 Methodology 

This chapter proposes the Task Decomposition Framework for real-world whole-aircraft level health Diagnosis of general aviation aircraft. Addressing the high noise, signal sparsity, and multi-task coupling characteristics inherent to the NGAFID dataset, we explicitly decouple end-to-end Diagnosis into AD (Global Discrimination) and FC (Local Identification), establishing a Meta-Architecture Specification grounded in the structural divergence of their receptive field requirements. Following a progressive logic of "theory–architecture–implementation–explanation": 

Section 3.1 formally establishes the mathematical foundations of the decomposition framework, demonstrating the intrinsic conflict between global discrimination and local analysis at the feature-granularity level; 

Section 3.2 accordingly designs the LMSD meta-architecture, elucidating the two-stage processing logic of "global screening and local precise diagnosis" together with decoupled training strategy; 

Section 3.3 instantiates the meta-architecture, detailing the technical implementations of the global-feature-oriented ConvTokMHSA component and the local-feature-oriented MMK Net component; 

Section 3.4 introduces KEL based on knowledge distillation, furnishing a post-hoc Interpretability mechanism for model decisions. This chapter thereby constructs a complete technical system spanning from abstract framework to concrete implementa-tion, offering a novel methodological perspective for diagnosis tasks in complex industrial scenarios. 

3.1 Task Formalization and Decomposition Framework Theory 

To address the inherent complexity of real-world whole-aircraft health diagnosis, we first establish a mathematical formalization that reveals the fundamental limitations of end-to-end modeling—specifically, the receptive field paradox between global operational discrimination and local fault characterization. Building upon this analysis, we propose the theoretical framework of task decomposition that explicitly decouples these heterogeneous objectives. Traditionally, Diagnosis tasks have been modeled as an end-to-end mapping fD : X → RN +1 , where the input space 

X ⊆ RL×D (L denotes sequence length, D denotes sensor dimension), and the probability distribution output by the diagnosis model is y = fD (x) = [ y0, y 1, . . . , y N ]⊤ ∈ RN +1 . Under this framework, y0 represents the probability of healthy state, while yi (i = 1 , . . . , N ) represents the probability of the i -th fault class. As illustrated in Figure .3(a), existing end-to-end paradigms feed the raw sample pool (containing mixed healthy/anomalous data) directly into a unified diagnosis model, simultaneously accomplishing health discrimination and fault classification through the single mapping fD .However, such modeling approaches overlook the essential characteristic of aviation Diagnosis tasks: Conditional Dependency. Specifically, the input sample x is essentially formed by feature concatenation of the monitoring data profile m ∈ RL×Dm (containing engine parameters, cylinder status, electrical indicators, and other information directly reflecting system physical states) and the operational pattern variable profile o ∈ RL×Do (containing flight status parameters IAS/VSpd/AltMSL, environmental parameter OAT, and other contextual information reflecting piloting styles, mission phases, and meteorological conditions), i.e., x = [ m ∥ o] ∈ RL×D , where Dm + Do = D. Therefore, the essence of Diagnosis tasks is not simple sample classification, but rather Conditional Classification—the model needs to learn the posterior distribution P (by|m, o). If operational conditions o are ignored and discrimination is based solely on monitoring data m , the model is highly susceptible to misidentifying normal responses under specific operational patterns (such as normal climbs under extreme weather) as fault manifestations, falling into spurious correlations [ 56 ], thereby misjudging normal operational patterns as faults. This dependency on operational context determines that Diagnosis tasks must integrate cross-temporal contextual information across the full sequence scope to accurately identify current operational patterns and detect systematic deviations. Furthermore, we decompose Diagnosis into two subtasks with explicit semantic interfaces. The first subtask is AD: 

fAD : X → R2, outputting the probability distribution pAD = [ ph, p a]⊤ (where ph is the healthy probability, pa

This task inherits the aforementioned essence of conditional discrimination—requiring identification of the current operational pattern o within full-sequence context, thereby determining whether monitoring data m deviates from the normal envelope under that pattern. Therefore, AD tasks rely on full-sequence receptive fields to integrate cross-temporal context, accurately identify current operational patterns, and detect systematic deviations on this basis. 6Task Decomposition for Aircraft Health Diagnosis The second subtask is FC: fF C : Xa → RN , where Xa = {x | fAD (x) = 1a} denotes the subset of samples determined as anomalous through the AD stage, outputting probability distribution pF C = [ p1, p 2, . . . , p N ]⊤ (pi is the probability of the i-th fault class), and ultimately obtaining class decision bc ∈ { 1, . . . , N } through argmax. This task requires identifying specific fault patterns on samples already confirmed as anomalous, essentially constituting a Conditional Fine-grained Classification problem, relying on the discriminative extraction of locally sensitive feature differences. Unlike AD, FC tasks focus on fault-related local temporal segments without needing to distinguish global operational patterns, because anomalous samples have already been screened through the global discrimination stage, with their "anomalousness" already confirmed under the corresponding operational pattern. These subtasks exhibit competing Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) maximization objectives: AD tasks require global SNR maximization, necessitating receptive fields that theoretically span the full sequence length L to integrate cross-temporal contextual information, accurately identify operational patterns, and suppress random disturbances. Conversely, FC tasks require local SNR maximization, with optimal receptive fields significantly smaller than L to isolate fault-related temporal segments and suppress cross-stage coupling noise. This receptive field paradox renders single architectures fundamentally incapable of simultaneous optimization: global attention mechanisms introduce excessive operational heterogeneity noise into FC's fine-grained discrimination, while local constraints sacrifice critical global context and pattern discrimination capabilities essential for AD. The irreconcilable nature of these requirements necessitates architectural-level explicit separation rather than implicit trade-offs within monolithic models. Based on this, this study proposes the Meta-Architecture Specification of DDF. Optimization-level hard isolation: Parameters θH and θF of the two-stage models are updated independently, with gradient backpropagation strictly prohibited from crossing the routing boundary. This hard isolation prevents fine-grained gradients from the fault classification stage from interfering with global pattern learning in the anomaly detection stage, and vice versa. During the inference phase, pre-trained HθH and FθF are loaded with parameters frozen, executing exclusively forward propagation. This training set decomposition represents one instantiation of the DDF meta-architecture, not its exclusive realiza-tion. While DDF accommodates both loosely-coupled (external cascading with separate training, as in LMSD) and tightly-coupled (single-model end-to-end) pattern, LMSD's strict decoupling offers distinct engineering advantages: independent AD and FC workflows enable precise alignment of data scale, model complexity, and computational resources. 

Output specification and decision mechanism: During training process, HθH and FθF output 2D and N-dimensional logit vectors ( zAD and zF C ), respectively, supervised via cross-entropy loss with one-hot labels. During inference, argmax operations replace threshold-based hard decisions, ensuring numerical stability and cross-task comparable confidences while eliminating threshold sensitivity. 

Heterogeneous hyperparameter configuration: Divergent architectural designs (hidden dimensions, encoder depths, learning rates) accommodate the differential demands of global context modeling versus local feature extraction, reinforcing the optimization matching between large-sample lightweight and small-sample complex models. 3.3.2 MMK Net: Micro-Scale Local Feature Extraction 

As the implementation backbone of FθF , MMK Net is specifically designed for precise extraction of fault features at the micro-scale. Diverging from the sequence tokenization employed by the Health Analyzer, MMK Net processes raw sequences directly, achieving high discriminative power under restricted receptive fields through micro-kernel design and information fidelity structures. Multi-Micro Kernel Convolution constitutes the core unit of the network. Each MMK Block employs a bottleneck layer (1×1 convolution) for dimensionality reduction, followed by parallel 1D convolution branches with kernel sizes of 1, 3, and 5 to extract local features at different scales. Output channels are concatenated and subsequently passed through Layer Normalization (LayerNorm) and ReLU activation. This small-kernel design (maximum size of 5) ensures strictly restricted receptive fields, preventing the introduction of global noise; the multi-scale parallelization captures fault feature manifestations across varying temporal granularities. 3.4 Keyness Extraction Mechanism via Distillation Learning 

To support subsequent interpretability analysis, this section introduces KEL, a lightweight, model-agnostic interpretabil-ity module that can be prepended to any model. Unlike interpretability methods relying on internal model attention, KEL simulates the model's shallow-layer attention distribution over input time series through external distillation learning, extracting sample-level keyness information for interpretability. Given an input sample x ∈ RB×L×D, KEL first generates a Temporal Keyness Vector wK ∈ RL/s via downsampling, characterizing the model's relative attention to individual temporal segments, where s denotes the temporal resolution control parameter. Specifically, a lightweight network Fenc comprising two cascaded CNN+ReLU layers maps x to raw keyness responses, subsequently normalized to the [0 , 1] interval via the sigmoid function σ to obtain keyness weights: 11 Task Decomposition for Aircraft Health Diagnosis 

wK = σ(Fenc (X; θK )) ∈ RB×(L/s ) (5) To match the original input dimensions, wK is upsampled to the original sequence length via the Temporal Expansion operation E(·) and broadcast to all feature channels, yielding the keyness matrix K:

K = E(wK ; s) ∈ RB×L×D (6) where E denotes repeat-interpolation. The weighted input Xkw = X ⊙ K is then fed into the downstream model. This strategy combining patch-level keyness generation with sequence-level expansion reduces KEL's computational overhead while preserving fine-grained temporal Interpretability. Notably, KEL employs ReLU activation to enforce non-negativity of Fenc outputs before sigmoid normalization, constraining the keyness lower bound to 0.5. This design stems from the Interpretability requirements of aviation PHM tasks: unlike traditional attention mechanisms that completely suppress non-attended regions via near-zero weights, KEL aims to identify temporal segments that are relatively more critical from the model's perspective rather than absolutely neglecting regions. Setting the keyness floor at 0.5—a neutral baseline—preserves the model's information accessibility to all temporal segments while highlighting the discriminative value of critical periods through relative weight differences. Directly prepending the keyness layer and training end-to-end risks disrupting original data distributions or overfitting to pretrained biases. 4.1 Data Processing 

Building upon the NGAFID dataset exposition (Section 2) and task formalization (Section 3.1), experiments are conducted on both the benchmark subset (Subset, 19 classes, 11,446 flights) and the complete dataset (Overall, 36 classes, 28,935 flights)—representing controlled validation scenarios versus open-environment high-complexity real-world conditions, respectively. 

Preprocessing pipeline: Missing values are addressed via Forward Fill (leading defaults set to zero). Segments shorter than 60 seconds or with sensor channel missing rates exceeding 10% are excluded, retaining only complete 23-dimensional records. Cubic Spline Interpolation normalizes variable-length segments to a fixed length of 2048. While interpolation may attenuate high-frequency information during takeoff/landing, this trade-off is acceptable given that cruise-phase weak fault detection presents the primary challenge. 

Validation protocol: Stratified 5-Fold Cross-Validation is employed with physical isolation between folds via file storage. For end-to-end models, four folds are used for training (9:1 split for training/internal validation), with the remaining fold held out for final evaluation. Z-score normalization parameters are computed exclusively from the training set and fixed prior to application on validation/test sets, preventing statistical leakage. 

Class imbalance mitigation: To address long-tailed distributions in Diagnosis and FC tasks, proportional replication data augmentation is employed to mitigate class imbalance. Let Ncmax denote the maximum class sample count and Nc

the original sample count for class c; in the meantime, the augmented sample count Ncda is calculated as: 

Ncda = min( kda × Nc, N cmax ) (10) where kda is the replication factor. This strategy prevents distribution distortion from excessive replication of tail classes while ensuring the model learns the essential characteristics of each category. 

4.2 Experimental Setup 4.2.1 Training Protocol Distinction 

Upon completion of the aforementioned data processing pipeline, this study designs distinct training protocols for conventional end-to-end methods and the LMSD decoupled architecture. For conventional end-to-end methods (e.g., Bi-LSTM, InceptionTime, ConvTokMHSA), training proceeds directly based on the 5-fold partitioning defined in Section 4.1: network weights are initialized independently for each fold, with internal validation loss monitored; the patience coefficient is set to 3, triggering early stopping if validation loss fails to decrease for 3 consecutive epochs. For LMSD, a Same-Origin Heterogeneous-Split decoupled training protocol is adopted: for each data fold, the Health Analyzer and Fault Analyzer share the complete training data of that fold, but independently execute a 9:1 training/validation split internally (analogous to independent sampling of base learners in ensemble learning). This design ensures mutual isolation of validation sets between the two modules, causing early stopping and learning rate adjustments to respond exclusively to the convergence characteristics of their respective tasks; simultaneously, the test sets for both modules remain strictly consistent (i.e., the test set of that fold) to ensure impartiality of integrated evaluation during the Diagnosis phase. This strategy significantly improves training data utilization compared to mandatory shared validation sets, while maintaining optimization independence across both stages, without incurring data leakage. 

4.2.2 Evaluation Metric System 

To comprehensively assess the applicability of the task decomposition framework in real-world industrial scenarios, a four-dimensional evaluation system is established covering traditional classification performance, training efficiency, inference overhead, and task-specific safety metrics. 

Traditional Classification Metrics: Accuracy (ACC) and macro-averaged F1-score (F1) serve as foundational discriminative indicators. For end-to-end Diagnosis tasks, Weighted F1 (WF1) is additionally introduced to account for sample proportion discrepancies across fault categories. 

Training Efficiency: Given the practical demands for model iterative training and rapid deployment in real-world aviation PHM scenarios, training efficiency constitutes the primary concern of this study. Specifically, Epoch Training

It is worth emphasizing that the construction of MCWPM constitutes a formalized mapping of the "Safety-over-Economy" engineering ethics prevalent in the aviation PHM domain—"better false alarms than missed detections." In real-world operational scenarios, missed detection (False Negative) implies flying with faults, directly endangering aviation safety, with risk costs far exceeding those of unnecessary maintenance incurred by false alarms (False Positive). Therefore, through differentiated penalty coefficients ( αP > β P ), MCWPM explicitly encodes safety constraints into computable optimization objectives. This is not intended to artificially inflate the apparent performance of specific methods, but rather to ensure that any model performing well on this metric must first satisfy the aviation industry's rigid requirements for extremely low missed detection rates. Even though this weighting strategy may disadvantage models emphasizing overall accuracy, it faithfully reflects risk weight allocations in real industrial scenarios, thereby guaranteeing domain validity and engineering credibility of the evaluation standards. In this study, αP =2.5 and βP =1.0, i.e., false alarms incur no penalty. 

4.2.3 Optimization Configuration 

This study adopts an adaptive learning rate scheduling strategy: the initial learning rate is model-dependent, halving immediately upon stagnation of target loss decrease, with a minimum learning rate floor of 5×10 -12 . The Adam optimizer (with β1 = 0 .9, β 2 = 0 .999 ) is selected for all tasks, with cross-entropy loss uniformly employed. Upon completion of testing for each fold, relevant metrics are automatically computed to ensure evaluation consistency. 

4.2.4 Randomness Control 

To avoid performance inflation from single favorable random seeds, this study adopts a stratified fixed-internal random hybrid randomness control strategy: the 5-fold data partitioning phase fixes random seeds to ensure reproducibility of sample distributions and physical isolation states; the internal training phase within each fold does not fix seeds, to assess the model's genuine sensitivity to parameter initialization. Furthermore, each fold independently repeats experiments for 3 rounds, with the median performance of that fold taken as the representative value; the Median-of-Medians across five folds is ultimately reported as the overall performance. This design totals 15 independent experimental rounds per model (5 folds × 3 rounds), striking a balance between computational overhead and statistical robustness: it precludes deliberate selection of favorable initializations while suppressing extreme fluctuations through median aggregation, truthfully reflecting the average robustness of models under actual deployment scenarios, in accordance with the rigor requirements of industrial-grade AI system evaluation.

4.6 Performance Ceiling on NGAFID 

While LMSD significantly outperforms end-to-end baselines, its absolute metrics remain below idealized datasets (e.g., C-MAPSS)—not due to architectural limitations, but the inherent discriminability ceiling imposed by NGAFID's authenticity and uncertainty. Figure. 6 illustrates these boundaries via ConvTokMHSA five-fold validation on the Subset. Figure. 7(a) classifies samples by stability across 10 rounds: Always Correctly Classified (correct in all 10 rounds, green), Generally Correctly Classified (correct in >5 but <10 rounds, blue), Frequently Misclassified (correct in >0 but <5 rounds, yellow), and Always Misclassified (incorrect in all 10 rounds, red). Figure. 7(b) further reveals misclassification patterns through cosine similarity to the 10 nearest healthy neighbors (excluding self). False Negatives (faults misclassified as healthy) exhibit extreme similarity to the healthy centroid (0.9746±0.0039), virtually indistinguishable from normal samples (0.9751±0.0052), displaying "camouflaged" charac-teristics; conversely, False Positives (healthy misclassified as faults) show significantly lower similarity (0.9533±0.0084), representing operational outliers. While Section 4.5 confirmed the model analyzes essential fault features, scarce samples for certain operational patterns impede learning their normal criteria—consistent with, rather than contradictory to, Section 4.5's conclusions. This disparity between "confusability" and "outlierness" reflects three fundamental dataset constraints: 

Label noise from maintenance records. NGAFID's retrospective labeling (anomalous = pre-maintenance, healthy = post-maintenance) induces False-Negatives (latent defects in "healthy" flights) and False-Positives (preventive vs. actual faults). This aleatoric uncertainty, compounded by epistemic uncertainty from scarce samples, fundamentally constrains end-to-end prognostic reliability [ 53 ]. DDF heterogeneously attenuates this: AD trains on full data for robustness, while FC—restricted to the anomalous subset—suffers amplified noise, limiting tail-class learning. 

Small samples and extreme long-tail distribution. With only 28,935 flights across 36 categories spanning three orders of magnitude (head >4,000 vs. tail <20), proportional replication ( kda = 3 ) merely interpolates within the original manifold without expanding tail-class learnability. MMK Net achieves <40% recall for categories with <30 samples, indicating fundamental data volume constraints. 23 Task Decomposition for Aircraft Health Diagnosis

Limited sensor capacity and feature sparsity. The Cessna-172's 23-dimensional 1Hz acquisition pales against modern QAR systems, submerging fault features in process noise. PCA confirms discriminative signals concentrate in the 12th to 18th minor components (low-variance subspaces). While LMSD's receptive field separation mitigates cross-stage noise, it cannot algorithmically overcome the physical information bottleneck imposed by sensor sparsity. In summary, NGAFID's authenticity imposes a tri-coupled performance ceiling; LMSD's metrics approach the discrim-inability boundary under current constraints. Future breakthroughs require: (1) Refined maintenance semantic annotation; (2) Enhanced flight parameter quality and capacity; (3) "Component-subsystem-whole-aircraft" multi-level architectures with physics-informed constraints, integrat-ing high-fidelity component models into deployable system-level solutions. These directions constitute the critical path from "usable" to "reliable" whole-aircraft aviation PHM. 

# 5 Conclusion and Future Work 

To address data uncertainty, task heterogeneity, and computational efficiency in general aviation health diagnosis, this paper proposes DDF and its instantiation, LMSD. Through "long-range global screening and micro-scale local precise diagnosis" heterogeneous cascading, DDF explicitly decouples end-to-end Diagnosis tasks. This framework resolves the receptive field paradox between global discrimination and local feature extraction, reduces computational overhead via Decoupled Training, and enhances physical traceability through two-stage interpretability. The core contribution is a methodological framework for whole-aircraft aviation PHM. Unlike end-to-end approaches that conflate health discrimination and fault characterization—causing representation learning conflicts—DDF archi-24 Task Decomposition for Aircraft Health Diagnosis tecturally separates these functions: lightweight global analyzers with full-sequence receptive fields learn operational patterns from large-scale data, while complex micro-scale analyzers with restricted fields extract local fault features from anomalous samples. This "large-sample lightweight" versus "small-sample complex" optimization reduces training time while ensuring stringent missed detection constraints through hard-threshold routing, formalizing aviation's "safety-first" ethics into AI design. NGAFID validation reveals both effectiveness and boundaries. LMSD achieves 4–8% MCWPM improvement over baselines under class imbalance and safety constraints, leveraging global attention for AD and micro-convolutional sensitivity for FC. Keyness visualizations confirm physical rationality: the Health Analyzer captures systematic deviations at critical transitions (e.g., pre-takeoff), while the Fault Analyzer localizes microscopic fault segments, achieving hierarchical cognition from "whether" to "which." However, label noise, extreme long-tail distributions, and limited sensor capacity impose performance ceilings, indicating that methodological advances must coordinate with data infrastructure improvements. Future whole-aircraft PHM development requires advances in three dimensions: (1) Methodological : Evolving from hard-threshold to uncertainty-quantified soft routing for adaptive feature fusion, and integrating physics-informed embeddings for causal reasoning; (2) Data : Enhancing flight parameter capacity through increased sensor dimensions/sampling rates, refined maintenance annotations, and "component-subsystem-whole-aircraft" multi-level acquisition; (3) Engineering : Adapting LMSD to edge computing constraints, enabling online incremental learning, and building cross-domain transfer capabilities. Ultimately, aviation PHM intelligence requires deep integration of aeronautical engineering, data science, and operations management to progress from post-event diagnosis toward predictive maintenance and autonomous health management, ensuring safety and trustworthiness throughout.
