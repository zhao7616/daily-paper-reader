# Feature Weighting Improves Pool-Based Sequential Active Learning for Regression 

Dongrui Wu, Fellow, IEEE 

Abstract —Pool-based sequential active learning for regression (ALR) optimally selects a small number of samples sequentially from a large pool of unlabeled samples to label, so that a more accurate regression model can be constructed under a given labeling budget. Representativeness and diversity, which involve computing the distances among different samples, are important considerations in ALR. However, previous ALR approaches do not incorporate the importance of different features in inter-sample distance computation, resulting in sub-optimal sample selection. This paper proposes three feature weighted single-task ALR approaches and two feature weighted multi-task ALR approaches, where the ridge regression coefficients trained from a small amount of previously labeled samples are used to weight the corresponding features in inter-sample distance computation. Experiments showed that this easy-to-implement enhancement almost always improves the performance of four existing ALR approaches, in both single-task and multi-task regression prob-lems. The feature weighting strategy may also be easily extended to stream-based ALR, and classification algorithms. 

Index Terms —Active learning, ridge regression, feature weight-ing, greedy sampling 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

In many real-world machine learning problems, acquiring a large amount of unlabeled data is relatively easy, but obtaining their labels is challenging, as it may be: 1) Labor intensive . For example, in affective computing [1], [2], it is easy to collect affective input signals such as sound, images, videos, etc., but labelling each piece of such signals may require the aggregation of individual labels from tens or more evaluators, due to the uncer-tainty and subtleness of emotions: the VAM ( Vera am Mittag in German, Vera at Noon in English) corpus [3] used 6-17 evaluators for each speech utterance, the DEAP (Database for Emotion Analysis using Physiolog-ical Signals) dataset [4] used 14-16 assessors for each video clip, the SEED-IV (Shanghai Jiao Tong University Emotion EEG Dataset) dataset [5] used 44 evaluators for each film clip, the GAPED (Geneva Affective Pic-ture Database) dataset [6] used 60 college students for each picture, and the IADS-2 (International Affective Digitized Sound, 2nd Edition) dataset [7] used over 110 assessors for each piece of sound. 2) Time consuming . For example, in fracturing based en-hanced oil recovery in the oil and gas industry [8],

180-day post-fracturing cumulative oil production is usually used as a performance measure. The fracturing parameters of an oil well, e.g., location, length of perforations, number of zones/holes, volumes of injected slurry/water/sand, etc., can be easily recorded; however, to get the groundtruth output (180-day post-fracturing cumulative oil production), one has to wait at least 180 days, which is very time consuming. 3) Expensive . Usually one needs to pay to recruit tens or hundreds of evaluators for the affective sound/images/videos, so there is considerable cost to label each piece of affective input. Fracturing an oil well and observing its 180-day cumulative oil production is even more expensive. So, an important and realistic question is: how to achieve the best possible learning performance from a small number of labeled samples? 

Different strategies have been proposed for this purpose, e.g., active learning [9], transfer learning [10], [11], self-supervised learning [12], their combinations [13]–[16], and so on. This paper considers pool-based sequential active learning, which optimally and sequentially selects a small number of samples from a large pool of unlabeled samples to label, so that a more accurate machine learning model can be constructed under a given (small) labeling budget. There have been many pool-based sequential active learning approaches proposed for classification problems [9], [17], e.g., uncertainty sampling, expected model change maximiza-tion, query-by-committee, etc. However, pool-based sequential active learning for regression (ALR) problem is much less studied. As an evidence, the deep active learning survey [17] published in 2025 includes 205 references, among which only one is on ALR. This paper focuses on the under-explored pool-based sequential ALR problem. Previous research [18] has proposed three important criteria to be considered in pool-based sequential ALR for selecting the next unlabeled sample to label: 1) Informativeness , i.e., the selected sample must contain rich information, which could be measured by the dis-agreement among multiple learners [19], or expected model change [20], etc. 2) Representativeness , i.e., the selected sample should rep-resent a large population, instead of being an outlier. 3) Diversity , i.e., all selected samples together should scat-ter across the full input space, instead of occupying only a small local region. Based on these three criteria, several ALR approaches

have been proposed, e.g., query-by-committee [19], greedy sampling [21], expected model change maximization [20], representativeness-diversity (RD) [18], greedy sampling in the input space (GSx) [22], greedy sampling in the output space [22], improved greedy sampling (iGS) in both input and output spaces [22], and the multi-task iGS (MT-iGS) [23]. Many of them, e.g., RD, GSx, iGS and MT-iGS, need to compute the distances among different samples when calculating the representativeness and/or diversity measures. However, their distance computation does not consider the weights associated with the input features (which can be estimated from the small amount of labeled samples), resulting in sub-optimal performance. This paper proposes a simple yet effective remedy, showing that by incorporating the estimated feature weights in distance computation, ALR performance can be consistently improved. Our main contributions are: 1) We propose three feature weighted (FW) single-task ALR approaches, i.e., FW-RD, FW-GSx and FW-iGS, to improve RD, GSx and iGS, respectively. 2) We propose two feature weighted multi-task (FW-MT) ALR approaches, i.e., FW-MT-GSx and FW-MT-iGS, to improve GSx (which can be used in both single task ALR and multi-task ALR) and MT-iGS, respectively. 3) We perform extensive experiments to demonstrate that each of the five feature weighted ALR approaches outperformed its corresponding unweighted version, and feature weighting benefits both linear and nonlinear prediction models. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows: Sec-tion II introduces four existing pool-based sequential ALR approaches, i.e., RD, GSx, iGS and MT-iGS, which need to compute the distances among different samples. Section III proposes five feature weighted ALR approaches, FW-RD, FW-GSx, FW-iGS, FW-MT-GSx, and FW-MT-iGS. Sections IV and V perform experiments to demonstrate the effective-ness and robustness of the single-task and multi-task feature weighted ALR approaches, respectively. Finally, Section VI draws conclusions and points out future research directions. II. R ELATED WORK

A. RD 

RD [18] considers both representativeness and diversity, as its name suggests. It first selects simultaneously d+1 unlabeled samples from the pool to label, so that a ridge regression model can be initialized. Then, it selects one sample to label in each subsequent iteration, and updates the ridge regression model accordingly, until M samples have been selected. In initialization, RD performs k-means ( k = d + 1 ) cluster-ing on the N unlabeled samples, and then selects from each cluster (to achieve diversity) the sample closest to its centroid (to achieve representativeness) for labeling. Assume m (m ≥ d + 1 ) samples have been selected and labeled. To select the (m + 1) -th sample to label, RD first performs k-means ( k = m + 1 ) clustering on all N samples. Since there are only m labeled samples, there must exist at least one cluster that does not contain any labeled samples. RD then identifies the largest such cluster (to achieve diversity), and selects the sample closest to its centroid (to achieve representativeness) for labeling. This process is repeated until 

M samples are selected and labeled. The pseudo-code of RD is given in Algorithm 1.

III. F EATURE WEIGHTED ALR The four approaches introduced in the previous section all involve computing the distances among the samples, e.g., in 

k-means clustering and in (1); however, they do not take the importance of different features into consideration. As a result, the distances may be problematic: 1) Within a single feature, its value changes with differ-ent units, and hence the distances between two sam-ples also changes with units, which should not be the case. For example, in the UCI autoMPG dataset used in our experiments, the weight of a car can be measured in pounds or kilograms or tons (e.g., 3504 pounds = 1589.39 kilograms = 1.58939 tons) , which does not change its characteristics at all; however, us-ing 3504/1589.39/1.58939 in computing the distances among different cars gives significantly different results (when other features keep the same), hence dramatically different representativeness and/or diversity values, and finally different sample selections. 2) Among different features, those with large magnitudes dominate the distance computation, whereas they may not be the most important features. 3) Some features may be redundant or irrelevant in real-world machine learning problems. Blindly using all features in distance computation is misleading, resulting in wrong representativeness and/or diversity values, and also suboptimal sample selections. So, we argue that the feature weights, which can be estimated from ridge regression or tree models, should be used to more accurately computing the distances, and hence to improve sample selection: 1) Within a single feature, when feature weight is con-sidered in distance computation, i.e., the value of the feature is replaced by (feature weight × feature value), the distances no longer change with the units of the features, and hence are more reasonable. 2) Among different features, when (feature weight × fea-ture value) is used in distance computation, the contri-butions of unimportant features with large magnitudes is suppressed by their small feature weights, avoiding misleading distances. 3) The contributions of redundant or irrelevant features to the distances may also be automatically adjusted by the feature weights, minimizing their negative impacts to sample selection. This section introduces our proposed five feature weighted ALR algorithms.

F. Discussion 

There are some other popular active learning algorithms, e.g., active learning with Gaussian processes [25], [26], Bayesian active learning by disagreement (BALD) [27], etc., which use uncertainty (informativeness) as the primary crite-rion for sample selection, and may also be used for regression problems with little or no modifications. However, these approaches may not be enhanced by our proposed feature weighting strategy, as they do not involve computing the distances among different samples (which is typically used in the diversity and representativeness measures), so feature weighting is unemployable. B. Algorithms 

We compared the performance of the following nine single-task ALR approaches on the 11 datasets in Table I: 1) BL , which is a baseline approach that randomly selects all M samples to label. 2) EMCM (expected model change maximization) [20], which considers only the informativeness, represented by expected model parameter changes, in sample selec-tion. 3) QBC (query-by-committee) [19], which considers only the informativeness, represented by model uncertainty, in sample selection. 4) RD , which is the RD ALR algorithm [18] introduced in Section II-A. 5) FW-RD , which is our proposed FW-RD ALR algorithm introduced in Section III-A. 6) GSx , which is the GSx ALR algorithm [22] introduced in Section II-B. 7) FW-GSx , which is our proposed FW-GSx ALR algo-rithm introduced in Section III-B. 8) iGS , which is the iGS ALR algorithm [22] introduced in Section II-C. 9) FW-iGS , which is our proposed FW-iGS ALR algo-rithm introduced in Section III-C. All nine approaches constructed a ridge regression model from the labeled samples. λ = 0 .1 was used in the objective function min  

> β

(‖y − Xβ‖2 + λ‖β‖2), where β contains the ridge regression bias and coefficients. 

C. Evaluation Process 

The evaluation process for single-task ALR approaches was identical to that used in [18]. For each dataset, let P be the pool of all samples. We first randomly selected 80% of the total samples as our training pool (denoted as P80 ), initialized the first d+ 1 labeled samples (for each dataset, d was the number of total features in Table I), identified one sample to label in each subsequent iteration by different algorithms, and built a ridge regression model. The maximum number of samples to be labeled, M , was 10% of the size of P80 . For large datasets, we clipped M at 60. After each iteration of each algorithm, we computed the root mean squared error (RMSE) and the correlation coefficient (CC) on the test set as the performance measures. As pointed out in [18], RMSE should be considered as the primary performance measure, because it is directly optimized in the objective function of ridge regression, whereas CC is not. Generally, as RMSE decreases, CC increases, but there could be exceptions. 

D. Main Results 

Fig. 1 shows the RMSEs and CCs of the nine single-task ALR algorithms on the 11 datasets, averaged over 100 runs. We can observe that: 1) Generally, as M (the number of labeled samples) in-creased, all nine algorithms achieved better performance (smaller RMSE and larger CC), which is intuitive. 2) EMCM , QBC , RD , GSx and iGS outperformed BL on almost all datasets, suggesting the effectiveness of ALR. 3) Generally, the feature weighed ALR approaches out-performed their unweighted counterparts, i.e., FW-RD 

outperformed RD , FW-GSx outperformed GSx , and 

FW-iGS outperformed iGS , demonstrating the effec-tiveness of feature weighting. As in [18], we also define an aggregated performance measure called the area under the curve (AUC) for the average RMSE and the average CC on each of the 11 datasets in Fig. 1. Fig. 2(a) shows the AUCs of the RMSEs, where the AUC of 

BL was used to normalize the AUCs of the other eight ALR algorithms for each dataset (so the AUC of BL was always 1). For the RMSE, a smaller AUC is preferred. Similarly, Fig. 2(b) shows the normalized AUCs of the CCs, where a larger AUC is preferred. The detailed numerical values are given in Table II. We can observe that: 1) On average, all eight ALR approaches achieved smaller RMSEs and larger CCs than BL , suggesting the effec-tiveness of ALR. 2) On average, the feature weighed ALR approaches out-performed their unweighted counterparts, demonstrating again the effectiveness of feature weighting. 3) Generally, feature weighting maintained the rank of the original ALR approaches. For example, in Fig. 2(a) the average normalized AUCs of the RMSEs of the unweighted ALR approaches were in the order of

F. Irrelevant Features G. Sensitivity to the Ridge Parameter λ

The feature weights are influenced by the ridge regression regularization parameter λ. This subsection studies the sensi-tivity of the feature weighted ALR approaches to λ.Using EE-Cooling as an example, Fig. 4 shows the RMSEs and CCs of the seven single-task ALR approaches (excluding QBC and EMCM), when λ ∈ { 0.01 , 0.1, 1, 10 }. The benefits of feature weighting are obvious: 1) For all λ considered, feature weighting always im-proved the performance of the ALR approaches, e.g., 

FW-RD outperformed RD , FW-GSx outperformed GSx ,and FW-iGS outperformed iGS .2) Though the performance of RD was generally worse than BL , by incorporating feature weighting, FW-RD 

outperformed BL , or at least achieved comparable per-formance. 

H. Effect of Different Number of Initially Labeled Samples 

Let nmin be the number of initially labeled samples. Though in all previous single-task ALR experiments, we always se-lected the first nmin = d + 1 unlabeled samples using RD or GSx, and then labeled them to initialize a ridge regression model, it should be noted that nmin can start from fewer than 

d + 1 samples, and this does not impact the effectiveness of feature weighting. Fig. 5 shows the RMSEs and CCs of iGS and FW-iGS 

on two representative datasets when nmin increased from 2 to 

d + 1 . We can observe that: 1) FW-iGS always outperformed the corresponding iGS ,regardless of nmin , suggesting again the effectiveness and robustness of feature weighting. 2) There was no consistent pattern on whether a smaller or larger nmin was better. For example, Fig. 5(a) shows that a smaller nmin may result in a smaller RMSE for 

FW-iGS , whereas Fig. 5(b) shows that a smaller nmin 

may result in the largest RMSE for FW-iGS .3) When M < d + 1 , both RMSE and CC exhibited large fluctuations as M changed. This is because ridge regression (and consequently the feature weights) is unstable when there are too few labeled samples (this is also the reason why we started from nmin = d + 1 in previous experiments). A remedy for small nmin is to choose a larger regularization parameter λ, as done in the next subsection. In summary, our proposed feature weighted FW-RD ,

FW-GSx and FW-iGS , and their original versions, can work with any nmin ≥ 2; however, which nmin is optimal is application dependent.

Since the number of initially labeled training samples was smaller than the feature dimensionality, we used a large λ = 10 for strong regularization. The results show that different initialization approaches resulted in different regression performance at the beginning: IRD was the best, and iRDM was the second best, both outperformed GSx. J. Nonlinear Regression Models 

In all previous experiments, the simple linear ridge re-gression model was used in both feature weight estimation and unlabeled sample prediction. This subsection studies two important problems: 1) Whether the feature weights estimated by a linear model is effective in nonlinear predictors? 2) Whether the feature weights estimated by a nonlinear model is more effective than those by a linear model? Fig. 7 shows the results when linear ridge regression was used to estimate the feature weights in FW-RD , FW-GSx 

and FS-iGS , and a nonlinear regression tree was trained for new sample prediction. The feature weighted ALR algorithms generally achieved better performance than or comparable performance with their unweighted counterparts, i.e., the fea-ture weights estimated by a linear model is also effective in nonlinear predictors. Fig. 8 shows the results when a nonlinear regression tree was used for both estimating the feature weights in FW-RD ,

FW-GSx and FS-iGS , and new sample prediction. Similar to Fig. 7, the feature weighted ALR algorithms generally achieved better performance than or comparable performance with their unweighted counterparts. Comparing Figs. 7 and 7, we cannot observe significant performance differences, i.e., the feature weights estimated by a linear model and a nonlinear model may be equally effective. These experiments demonstrated the effectiveness and ro-bustness of feature weighting: both linear and nonlinear regres-sion models could be used in feature weight estimation, and for new sample prediction; moreover, the regression models used in these two parts could be different. In any case, feature weighting rarely hurts the ALR performance. V. E XPERIMENT RESULTS ON MULTI -T ASK ALR Experiments are performed in this section to demonstrate the performance of the two proposed multi-task feature weighted ALR approaches. 

A. Datasets 

We used two multi-task regression datasets: 1) VAM , which contains spontaneous speech with authentic emotions recorded from guests in a German TV talk-show and has been used in many studies [3], [23], [31]–[34]. There are 947 emotional utterances from 47 speakers (11m/36f), each labeled in the 3D space of valence, arousal and dominance. The same 46 acoustic features extracted in previous research [23], [33], [34], including nine pitch features, five duration features, six energy features, and 26 Mel Frequency Cepstral Coefficient (MFCC) features, were used in this paper. Each feature was normalized to mean 0 and standard deviation 1. 2) Energy Efficiency (EE) 4, which aims to predict two outputs (the heating load and cooling load requirements) for 768 different building shapes simulated in Ecotect. There are eight numerical features, including relative compactness, surface area, wall area, roof area, overall height, orientation, glazing area, and glazing area distri-bution. EE-Cooling in Table I is part of this dataset. 

B. Algorithms 

We compared the performance of the following five multi-task ALR approaches on the VAM and EE datasets: 1) BL , which is a baseline approach that randomly selects all M samples to label. 2) GSx , which is the GSx ALR algorithm [22] introduced in Section II-B. It can be used in both single-task ALR and multi-task ALR. 3) FW-MT-GSx , which is our proposed FW-MT-GSx ALR algorithm introduced in Section III-D. 4) MT-iGS , which is the MT-iGS ALR algorithm [23] introduced in Section II-D. 5) FW-MT-iGS , which is our proposed FW-MT-iGS ALR algorithm introduced in Section III-E. All five approaches construct a ridge regression model from the labeled samples, with ridge parameter λ = 10 . The ridge parameter here was much larger, as all five approaches started from only 5 labeled samples, lower than the feature dimensionality, so a large λ was needed to enforce strong regularization. 

C. Evaluation Process 

The evaluation process for multi-task ALR approaches was identical to that used in [23]. For each dataset, 30% of the samples were randomly selected as the training pool, and the remaining 70% as the test pool. The number of selected samples ranged from 5 to 100 on VAM, and 5 to 40 on EE (the performance converged around M = 40 on EE).

E. Discussion 

The original multi-task ALR algorithms, GSx and MT-iGS ,prioritize balance across different tasks, potentially overlook-ing samples that are particularly informative for individual tasks. GSx and MT-iGS may be improved in this direction, i.e., to give important tasks more consideration; however, this is out of the scope of the current paper. However, no matter whether the multiple tasks have bal-anced or imbalanced importance, the effectiveness of feature weighting may not be affected. As an example, compare Fig. 1(g), where the task of EE-Cooling is considered without considering the task of EE-Heating at all (i.e., EE-Cooling has the maximum importance, whereas EE-Heating has the minimum importance), and Fig. 10, where both tasks of EE-Cooling and EE-Heating are considered equally important. In both cases, feature weighted ALR algorithms always outper-formed their unweighted counterparts. VI. C ONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE RESEARCH 

In many real-world machine learning problems, acquiring a large amount of unlabeled data is relatively easy, but obtaining their labels is labor intensive, time consuming, and/or expen-sive. Active learning is a promising strategy to cope with these challenges. Particularly, pool-based sequential ALR optimally selects a small number of samples sequentially from a large pool of unlabeled samples to label, so that a more accurate regression model can be constructed under a given (small) labeling budget. Representativeness and diversity, which in-volve computing the distances among different samples, are important considerations in ALR.
