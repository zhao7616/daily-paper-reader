Abstract 

Competing risks occur in survival analysis when multiple causes of death are present. They play a prominent role in several domains extending beyond biostatistics to encompass epidemiology, actuarial sciences, and reliability theory. This paper adopts a multi‚Äďstate modeling framework to competing risks. We introduce a class of flexible nonparametric priors, defined through hierarchical completely random measures, to model the transition probabilities, and identify the specific (conditionally) conjugate member of this general class. Furthermore, we determine the joint marginal distribution of the data and of a latent random partition, and characterize the posterior distribution of the model. Leveraging these distributional results, we evaluate the predictive probability that a future event is of a specific type (e.g. death from a particular cause), as a function of the time at which the event occurs. The resulting function, derived on sound principles, is termed the prediction curve , and represents a major innovation in the literature. In addition, we provide posterior estimates for the survival function, and for the cause‚Äďspecific incidence and subdistribution functions. Suitable simulation algorithms for posterior inference are also devised. The model‚Äôs performance, as well as the algorithms‚Äô effectiveness, is evaluated through simulation studies. Finally, we illustrate our approach on clinical datasets. 

Keywords: Bayesian Nonparametrics; Competing risks; Hierarchical Processes; Prediction Curve; Random Partition; Survival Analysis. 1

# 1 Introduction 

Survival analysis must often account for multiple competing sources of risk, where the observed failure (or death) is determined by one among several causes (or types of events). For instance, in clinical studies, a fatal outcome after treatment may be due to a number of different causes, that are either related or unrelated to the treatment, and only one of those causes can be actually observed. Competing events or competing risks arise when the observation of an event of interest, and of the associated survival time, is potentially prevented by the occurrence of one of a set of distinct alternative events (Kalbfleisch and Prentice, 2002; Geskus, 2024). The natural application of competing risks analysis is in biomedical and clinical studies, in which subjects may die from different causes. However, competing risks play a prominent role in several domains, another notable setting being reliability‚Äďtesting in industry, where the possible breakdown of a complex system is due to the failure of one of its components.

The statistical analysis of competing risks data typically focuses on: (i) the estimation of the cause‚Äďspecific cumulative incidence functions, which allows to estimate the probabilities that each competing event occurs within some time interval; (ii) the association of these quantities with different treatments or predictors of interest (Fine and Gray, 1999); (iii) the estimation of the survival function S in (2) . These statistical problems are widely discussed in the frequentist literature, as evidenced by numerous reviews and textbooks; see, e.g., 3Kalbfleisch and Prentice (2002), Lawless (2003), Crowder (2012), Geskus (2015, 2024), Cook and Lawless (2018). In contrast, the literature on Bayesian nonparametric approaches to competing risks is rather limited. Bayesian models are often based on gamma or beta process priors, which were successfully employed to univariate survival data (Dykstra and Laud, 1981; Lo and Weng, 1989; Hjort, 1990). Recently, Arf√© et al. (2019) introduced a beta‚ÄďStacy process generalization (Walker and Muliere, 1997) for cumulative incidence functions on a discrete timescale. Xu et al. (2016) proposed a model for evaluating dynamic treatment regimes based on dependent Dirichlet processes, while Xu et al. (2020) develop a similar approach for semi‚Äďcompeting risks. Instead, independent gamma processes are considered in Oganisian et al. (2024) for modelling sequential treatment decisions with time-varying confounders. Further pointers and discussions can be found in M√ľller and Quintana (2004); M√ľller et al. (2015). An alternative construction based on Bayesian Additive Regression Trees was proposed in Sparapani et al. (2020); we discuss and compare it to our method in Section S7, also through numerical illustrations. Unlike previous contributions, we take a comprehensive approach by specifying a prior on the transition probabilities of the associated multi‚Äďstate model, successfully addressing all major inferential problems in the competing risks literature. Additionally, our principled framework leads to a significant breakthrough in prediction: we introduce the concept of 

prediction curve , which corresponds to the conditional probability of a future event being of a specific type given the past observations, as a function of the event‚Äôs occurrence time. Prediction curves are not only of foundational and methodological interest, but also offer great potential for practical decision‚Äďmaking due to their clear interpretability.

# 3 Dependent prior specification 

We construct a tractable and effective dependent prior for ňúőľ = ( ňúőľ1, . . . , ňúőľD), inducing a prior on the space of cause-specific hazard rates and transition probabilities, as described in Section 2. Specifically, we adopt hierarchical structures, a popular approach for introducing dependence among completely random measures (CRMs). Then, we introduce the marked process, investigate some key prior properties, and provide a consistency result. An overview of CRMs is given in Section S2. 

## 3.1 Hierarchical completely random measures 

A prominent research stream in Bayesian Nonparametrics focuses on heterogeneous data or latent structures associated with different yet related experimental conditions. These settings can be framed within partial exchangeability, which assumes within‚Äďgroup homogeneity and 8across‚Äďgroup heterogeneity. Success stories of this approach include topic modeling, ANOVA, change‚Äďpoint detection, reliability analysis, species sampling and numerous biostatistics and bioinformatics applications (see Quintana et al., 2022, for a recent review). A popular strategy to model heterogeneity nonparametrically leverages vectors of dependent CRMs, as prior dependence among CRMs enables borrowing of information across different groups. Several effective proposals have appeared in the literature. In survival analysis, Lijoi and Nipoti (2014) model each vector component as a superposition of a common and an independent idiosyncratic CRMs; similar additive structures are employed in M√ľller et al. (2004) and Lijoi et al. (2014) to model dependent random probabilities. More complex constructions based on multivariate L√©vy intensities are found in Epifani and Lijoi (2010) and Riva-Palacio and Leisen (2018, 2021), relying on L√©vy copulas, and in Griffin and Leisen (2017), where compound random measures (CoRMs) are introduced. CoRMs represent a general class of dependent random measures encompassing both superpositions of CRMs (Lijoi and Nipoti, 2014; Lijoi et al., 2014) and weighted CRMs (Chen et al., 2013). See Riva-Palacio et al. (2022) for an application to survival regression models. Instead, Nipoti et al. (2018) define group‚Äďspecific baseline hazard rates in frailty models employing independent CRMs. Further Bayesian nonparametric constructions of dependent random measures with biological and medical applications, though not directly based on CRMs, can be found in Jara et al. (2010); Jara and Hanson (2011); Soriano and Ma (2019); Cassese et al. (2019); Christensen and Ma (2020). Unlike contributions above, we employ dependent random measures in an exchangeable framework to jointly model survival times Ti and competing causes ‚ąÜi. Specifically, we focus on hierarchical structures that satisfy a conditional independence assumption, paralleling the standard Bayesian paradigm arising from de Finetti‚Äôs representation theorem for exchangeable random elements. ## 6.2 Posterior conditional sampling of random measures 

Marginal algorithms cannot be used for uncertainty quantification around posterior estimates of functionals involving the random measures, as marginalizing over ňúőľ1, . . . , ňúőľD averages out the associated uncertainty. Indeed, the conditional estimates computed at each iteration as in Section 5.3 are not a sample from the posterior of the corresponding functionals, but rather of their conditional expectations, given the latent variables. To address this issue, one needs to generate samples from the actual posterior of the random measures: this properly accounts for posterior uncertainty and enables the computation of reliable credible bands. Specifically, at each iteration of the marginal algorithm, we sample independently from the posterior hCRMs, given the observations and latent variables. Based on these samples, valid posterior draws of functionals can be computed from their definitions (5) and (7). The practical implementation of this method requires sampling from the posterior hCRMs in Theorem 7. The Ferguson-Klass algorithm (Ferguson and Klass, 1972) is the most popular approach for generating realizations of CRMs, as it ensures their jumps are a.s. decreasing, allowing to control the associated approximation error. See Barrios et al. (2013) and references therein, and Br√ľck (2025) for a novel approach to sampling based on generative neural networks. For non-homogeneous CRMs, Wolpert and Ickstadt (1998) proposed a simpler alternative, which however does not generate a.s. decreasing jumps. Nevertheless, when non‚Äďhomogeneity is induced by exponential tilting of a homogeneous prior, as in our case, the sequence of jumps can still be upper‚Äďbounded by a decreasing sequence obtained from the prior (Camerlenghi et al., 2021). 78 S7 Comparison with a tree-based approach 

This section presents a comparison of the method proposed in our paper, based on hCRMs, with the nonparametric approach to competing risks of Sparapani et al. (2020). Their method relies on Bayesian Additive Regression Trees (BART) (Chipman et al., 2010), a tree‚Äďbased ensemble technique widely used for both classification and regression. Specifically, the authors adapted the binary probit BART developed in Sparapani et al. (2016) for discrete‚Äďtime survival analysis to the competing risks scenario. A software implementation of their method is publicly available as part of the BART package in R.Before presenting the main numerical results on synthetic and real data, we highlight several remarkable features that set the two modeling approaches apart. First, our model is based on kernel mixtures, and thus the prior is supported on the space of continuous cause‚Äďspecific hazards. As a result, our method is naturally suited to model continuous survival data. In contrast, the tree‚Äďbased approach in Sparapani et al. (2020) assumes discrete‚Äďtime survival data, since these can be easily accommodated within the BART framework. Additionally, while our approach naturally yields estimates of cause‚Äďspecific incidence functions and prediction curves, the tree‚Äďbased method is limited to estimating survival and subdistribution functions. Indeed, these are the functionals included in the output of the crisk.bart function. Moreover, introducing the BART method, the authors consider only two competing causes of risks, namely the main cause of interest and all the others together. Accordingly, the comparison focuses on scenarios involving two competing risks. Finally, categorical predictors are incorporated via a Cox proportional hazards model (Section 7.3), which preserves interpretability at the cost of reduced flexibility. In contrast, their proposal allows for a richer dependence structure. The comparison presented below is necessarily limited to the estimation of survival and subdistribution functions, since, as noted, the tree‚Äďbased method does not cover cause‚Äď specific incidence functions and prediction curves, which are a distinctive capability of our approach. The results should be viewed as preliminary, and we refrain from drawing definitive conclusions, as a more extensive analysis is required and will be the subject of future work.
