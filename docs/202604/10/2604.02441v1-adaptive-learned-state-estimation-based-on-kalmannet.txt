Abstract —Hybrid state estimators that combine model-based Kalman filtering with learned components have shown promise on simulated data, yet their performance on real-world au-tomotive data remains insufficient. In this work we present Adaptive Multi-modal KalmanNet (AM-KNet), an advancement of KalmanNet tailored to the multi-sensor autonomous driv-ing setting. AM-KNet introduces sensor-specific measurement modules that enable the network to learn the distinct noise characteristics of radar, lidar, and camera independently. Ahypernetwork with context modulation conditions the filter on target type, motion state, and relative pose, allowing adaptation to diverse traffic scenarios. We further incorporate a covariance estimation branch based on the Josephs form and supervise it through negative log-likelihood losses on both the estimation error and the innovation. A comprehensive, component-wise loss function encodes physical priors on sensor reliability, target class, motion state, and measurement flow consistency. AM-KNet is trained and evaluated on the nuScenes and View-of-Delft datasets. The results demonstrate improved estimation accuracy and tracking stability compared to the base KalmanNet, narrowing the performance gap with classical Bayesian filters on real-world automotive data. 

Index Terms —State Estimation, Kalman Filter, Tracking 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Advanced driver-assistant systems (ADAS) have greatly contributed to improved safety of vehicles on the road, pre-venting crashes, and reducing the number of accidents. In the current ADAS pipelines the Fusion of noisy measurements from Radar, Camera, and occasionally Lidar is a crucial step towards obtaining reliable and accurate tracks that can be used for motion planning. Classical fusion and state estimation methods are grounded in Bayesian estimation theory. The Kalman filter (KF) and its nonlinear extensions, the extended Kalman filter (EKF) and the unscented Kalman filter (UKF) are the most widely adopted. These methods model the target state as a stochastic process and provide explicit uncertainty quantification alongside the state estimate. With the increasing presence of deep neural networks (DNNs) and the availability of large datasets and compute resources, data-driven methods have been established in both research and industry approaches. Learning-based frameworks for ADAS often follow two paradigms. First, the end-to-end

(E2E) driving approach, which is characterized by an interface to a learned model that takes sensor measurements as input and provides trajectories or actuation as output. This approach is the least verifiable or interpretable, as there is no information on the scene or decision-making process, merely on the trajec-tory output. The second paradigm is the modular stack, which often retains the classical ADAS structures of perception ,

prediction , planning , but uses learned, data-driven modules. The modular paradigm exposes intermediate representations and outputs like objects or lanes, but can still be trained jointly with end-to-end objectives. Providing intermediate outputs increases the interpretability compared to the black-box E2E approach. However, the learned modules themselves generally lack interpretability. During the sensor fusion step this contrast is particularly stark when considering Bayesian methods which follow physical models and provide uncertainty measures. Classical, probabilistic methods of state estimation have ad-vantages through their underlying physical principles and ex-plainability provided by their inherent uncertainty awareness. However, DNNs are capable of extracting complex structures and relationships from data, leading to superior performance in many applications such as computer vision or natural language processing. For the task of state estimation, recurrent neural networks (RNNs) [1] or Transformer [2] architectures are used most commonly, as they are effective at modeling temporal dependencies in sequential data. In Deep Kalman Filters [3], the authors learn a latent state-space model directly from data, replacing traditional transi-tion and observation models. While their model proved that accurate data-driven estimation is feasible, the learned latent space and dynamics are unconstrained and depend strongly on the training data. Similarly, Deep Variational Bayes Fil-ters [4] learns a latent dynamical model without prior physical knowledge. The system learns Markovian latent state-space models from sequences and allows gradients to propagate through the transition model over time, facilitating dynamical information flow. Another approach to a neural state estimator was presented with Recurrent Kalman Networks [5], where the authors retained a KF-style recurrence with a Gaussian cell state and a prediction and update cycle. While these data-driven models demonstrated their ability to perform estimation tasks, the lack of interpretability and need for well-curated datasets motivated the development of systems that combine data-driven models with rule-derived

models to enhance the interpretability and physical knowledge of the latter with the capacity and adaptiveness of the former. One of the most prominent examples of such a system was introduced by Revach et al. [6]. They present KalmanNet, a state estimator that follows the Kalman Filter equations, but replaces the covariance and Kalman Gain computations with a recurrent neural network (RNN). The internal cell state of RNNs is used to estimate the covariances over the time sequence, while a Kalman Gain is output at each timestep. The authors aimed to preserve interpretability of a Kalman Filter while gaining the benefits of a data-driven model. An extension to KalmanNet has been proposed by Han et al. [7] who presents a Multi-Model KalmanNet that utilizes multiple KalmanNets with different motion models. The estimates of these KalmanNets are combined using weights predicted by a deep neural network, which learns, from historical state estimates and innovations, the similarity between the predicted and observed target motion. In MAML KalmanNet [8] the authors present model-agnostic meta-learning into the KalmanNet system to address the challenges of scarce labeled training data and inflexi-ble network architectures. It pre-trains the network param-eters on simulated data across different noise conditions, enabling quick adaption to new state-space models. Split-KalmanNet [9] addresses performance degradation due to model mismatch in the EKF by replacing the single RNN KalmanNet architecture with two RNNs that separately learn the predicted state covariance and innovation covariance. By using the measurement Jacobian, the predicted state covari-ance, and innovation covariance the Kalman Gain can be ex-plicitly computed, improving robustness under heterogeneous noise conditions. To address the covariance estimation with KalmanNet, Mortada et al. [10] separate the noise dependent term of the covariance update and predict its Cholesky factor using additional modules. To constrain the partially learned state covariance, the authors add a loss term which consists of the normalized estimation error squared (NEES) and the logarithm of the covariance matrix determinant, to prevent the covariance from blowing up. An alternative approach to covariance estimation is Bayesian KalmanNet [11], which leverages Concrete dropout to create an approximate Bayesian model ensemble from the stochastic KalmanNet parameters. At inference time the model ensemble yields a set of state es-timates from which the mean and covariance can be computed. The training is supervised through an empirical tracking loss and a Kullback-Leibler regularization term that constrains the learned dropout probability and dicourages degenerate dropout behavior. In Adaptive KalmanNet [12] the authors present a system that uses a hypernetwork and context modulation to adapt to different state space dynamics. Adaptive KalmanNet is trained by first training the KalmanNet module on a set of trajectories with similar noise statistics. Then the KalmanNet weights are frozen and the hypernetwork is trained on the full dataset with varying noise statistics. The authors evaluations showed their systems adaptibility to varying noise distributions. Although Revach et al. showed superior performance of KalmanNet [6] on simulated data and other evaluations, an in-depth evaluation [13] showed insufficient performance when using real-world automotive data. In this work we will address the performance gap and present an improved version of KalmanNet that is evaluated on two automotive datasets. Finally, sections VI and VII will present and discuss the results of the evaluation with section VIII concluding this work. II. P ROBLEM STATEMENT 

The evaluation of the base KalmanNet [6] on automotive radar data yielded lower accuracy and unstable tracking com-pared to rule-based filter [13]. The unstable tracking indicated that the base KalmanNet did not learn the statistics of the data and the underlying physical model of the target motion. In this work, we present Adaptive Multi-modal (AM-KNet), which is an adaption and advancement of KalmanNet for the automotive setting. We utilise two automotive datasets, namely nuScenes [14] and View of Delft [15] for development and evaluation of AM-KNet. We adapt the system to accept multi-modal data, extend the loss function to consider different modalities, motion states, and the flow of the target motion. Following the implementations in Adaptive-KalmanNet [12] we also add hypernetwork based context modulation. We change the context vector, which serves as input to the hypernetwork, to provide information on the target type, its motion-state, and relative pose to the ego vehicle. The new KalmanNet model is evaluated on the two datasets, where we compare the performance to a state of the art bayesian filter. III. R ELEVANT WORK

A. KalmanNet 

KalmanNet [6] is a hybrid approach that couples a classi-cal, model-based filtering structure with learned, data-driven modules. The authors present it as a method for real-time state estimation in nonlinear dynamical systems when only incomplete or approximate domain knowledge is available. In particular, KalmanNet is intended to operate with an ap-proximated system model while avoiding the need for explicit knowledge of the process noise vk and measurement noise 

wk. To achieve this, it employs a recurrent neural network (RNN) that is trained to infer the Kalman Gain (KG) directly from data. This RNN is embedded within the Kalman filtering routine, so KalmanNet still follows the familiar predict–update recursion. In the prediction step, (3) is used to form the prior state estimate ˆxk+1 |k and (5) is applied to obtain the pre-dicted measurement ˆzk+1 |k. Unlike the standard KF, however, KalmanNet does not propagate the second-order moment of the state. In the subsequent update step, it computes the poste-rior estimate ˆxk+1 |k+1 according to (9). Rather than explicitly calculating the KG via the state and innovation covariance matrices as in (7), KalmanNet replaces that computation with an RNN prediction of the gain Wk+1 . As a result, the method can be applied without explicit access to the state covariance matrix P or the innovation covariance matrix S.Because the classical KG expression in (7) depends on the state and innovation covariances, the RNN is fed with hand-designed features intended to reflect the underlying statistical information: 

F1 The observation difference ∆˜ zk = zk − zk−1

F2 The innovation difference ∆zk = zk − ˆzk|k−1

F3 The forward evolution difference ∆˜ xk = ˆ xk|k − ˆxk−1|k−1

F4 The forward update difference ∆ˆ xk = ˆ xk|k − ˆxk|k−1

Here, F3 measures the change between two successive posterior state estimates, whereas F4 captures the discrepancy between the posterior and prior estimates at the same time index. Therefore, at time k the state-related inputs available to the network are ∆˜ xk−1 and ∆ˆ xk−1. Analogously, F1 

quantifies the difference between consecutive observations, and F2 represents the innovation, i.e., the mismatch between the observation and its prediction. Interpreting these signals, 

F1 and F3 mainly describe temporal changes in observations and state estimates, while F2 and F4 emphasize residual terms associated with measurements and state updates. To mirror the recursive nature of Kalman filtering, the authors adopt an RNN-based design, motivated by the idea that the network’s internal memory can implicitly track covariance-like quantities over time [6]. Concretely, the KalmanNet module comprises three gated recurrent units (GRU) [17]. These GRUs are associated with three covariance terms: the process noise covariance Q, the prior state covariance Pk|k−1,and the innovation covariance Sk. The first two GRUs use a hidden dimension of m2, and the third uses n2. In addition, the GRUs are paired with dedicated fully connected layers at their inputs and outputs, and they are connected in a cascaded manner: the output of the Q GRU is provided as input to the 

Pk|k−1 GRU, whose output then feeds into the Sk GRU. The latter two representations are ultimately used to produce the KG estimate. For learning, KalmanNet is trained in a supervised fashion on the posterior state estimate ˆxk, rather than directly super-vising the network’s KG output. The objective is a least error loss: 

L = |xk − ˆxk|k|2 (11) Additional architectural and training details, along with the original implementation, can be found in the original KalmanNet work [6].

C. Adaptive KalmanNet 

To address the challenge of adaption to changing state space dynamics, Adaptive-KalmanNet [12] introduces context modulation [18] with a hypernetwork [19]. Hypernetworks are DNNs that predict the weights of another network. This concept was further developed by [18] to introduce context modulation, which does not predict the weight matrices, but instead applies an affine transformation on the pre-activations of a network. Adaptive-KalmanNet uses the concept of con-text modulation to adapt KalmanNet to different situations, providing a scalar state of world input, which can be seen as a noise ratio, to the hyper network. 

D. OAFuser 

The Online Adaptive Fuser (OAFuser) [20] is a tracking and fusion framework that estimates process and measurement noise covariances jointly with the object state. The system consists of four coupled estimators. An α-β filter provides a predicted measurement from which a measurement noise estimate R is derived through the outer product of its residual. A second α-filter tracks the innovation covariance M using the residual of the main estimator. From R, M , and the current state covariance, the process noise intensity is com-puted analytically and bounded to prevent unrealistic values. A standard Kalman filter then performs the state update with the online-estimated noise matrices. By adapting both R and 

Q at every time step, OAFuser adjusts its correction sensitivity to varying target dynamics and sensor conditions without requiring multiple parallel models. The authors demonstrated competitive tracking accuracy compared to an IMM filter while achieving a significantly lower computational footprint, making it suitable for real-time automotive applications. IV. M ETHODS 

The goal of KalmanNet is to learn second order mo-ments of the measurements, the dynamical model, and the state estimate. Each sensor has its own characteristics and properties, radar, for example, provides more accurate radial measurements but exhibits noiser data for lateral measure-ments, whereas camera detections generally provide excellent lateral accuracy but have a poor depth accuracy. Likewise, the divergence of the dynamical model, which is characterized by the process noise, depends on the motion state of the target. A vehicle moving in a straight line at a fixed velocity can be ideally modelled with the Constanct Velocity model, but other scenarios might require an adjusted process noise. AM-KNet is an advancement of KalmanNet with changed architecture and training strategy to improve estimation accu-racy and tracking performance. The architecture changes en-able KalmanNet to process data from different sensor modal-ities and receive the necessary information that is required by the complex autonomous driving use case. The comprehensive loss function introduces physical knowledge about the sensor modality and the target motion, facilitating KalmanNet to learn the dynamics of the scene and its objects. The following will provide a detailed explanation of the changes to model architecture and the loss function. 

A. Architecture 1) Multi-modal KalmanNet: To be suitable for real world applications in navigation and tracking, such as autonomous driving, a state estimator has to be able to process and fuse data from different information sources. A general overview of our system can be seen in Fig. 3 while a detailed view of AM-KNet can be seen in Fig. 1. The measurements are used to update the same KalmanNet in sequential order. If the measurements have different timestamps a prediction step is performed between the updates. The measurement space specific modules are marked in Fig. 1 with a read square, they process data related to the measurements and extract the noise statistics to compute the Kalman Gain. We create distinctive, measurement specific, sets of these modules for each sensor, during training and inference lidar data is always passed to the lidar-set of measurement specific modules, camera data to the camera-set and radar measurements to the radar-set. This change allows KalmanNet to learn the sensor and detection characteristics for each modalities. 

2) Motion State Awareness: In order to facilitate adap-tion to different motion types we provide AM-KNet with information on the current motion state relative to the ego vehicle. Based on an analysis of the measurement errors and measurement distributions in the datasets we chose Stationary ,

Same Lane , and Crossing to categorize the motion state of objects in the scene. Same Lane and Crossing imply moving targets, thus the three motion states are mutually exclusive. The motion state is detected based on position and the velocity vector and represented as a one-hot encoding with three states. This motion state encoding is passed to GRU Q and 

GRU P and used to gate the measurement features that are passed to FC 7 . The gating mechanism is also based on the analysis of the used dataset and reduces the impact of noisy measurements. While not relevant to motion states, a general distribution of detection errors can be seen in Fig. 2. 

3) Context Modulation: In an autonomous driving scenario a state estimator is confronted with different types of scenarios, targets, driving maneuvers, and sensor settings. To be able to handle this diverse environment we take inspiration from AdaptiveKalmanNet [12] and use a hypernetwork with context modulation to modulate the pre-activations of AM-KNet. Unlike AM-KNet, which only passes a scalar value into the hypernetwork to represent a noise ratio our implementation will receiver a richer context. Our hypernetwork consists of four fully connected layers with ReLU activation functions. The first layer FC H1 receives the 27 component input which contains encodings for the object type (Car, Truck, ...), motion state, and target pose. After FC H2 the modulation is ouput by two heads FC GAIN and FC SHIFT which provide the context modulation. This modulation is an affine transformation ap-plied to the pre-activations, i.e. the values after multiplication of inputs by weights and before application of the activation function. The shift is multiplied with the pre-activation and the gain added to it. During inference, each target, at each timestep will receive modulation from the hypernetwork based on its own context. 

4) Covariance Estimation: While the cell state of the GRUs in the KalmanNet architecture contain information on noises in the scene, prior evaluation [13] showed that these cannot be directly used as a covariance matrix. We follow the approach used in Recursive KalmanNet [10], where the authors use Josephs formula of covariance estimation, but combine the prediction and update of the state covariance and split them into two parts. One part uses the state space matrices, while the other, noise dependent term, is learned through a GRU.

A. State-Space Formulation 

AM-KNet uses a constant acceleration motion model augmented with extent and orientation states xt =[ x, y, v x, v y , a x, a y , l, w, c x, c y , ψ ]⊤ ∈ R11 , where 

(x, y ) denote the position, (vx, v y ) the velocity, and (ax, a y )

the acceleration of the object reference-point. The shape is characterised by length and width of the bounding box and the object yaw angle (l, w, ψ ), while (cx, c y ) give the center of the tracked object. The reference point is chosen to maximize observation by the sensors, it is the midpoint on the side of the target which faces the ego vehicle, taking the side lengths into consideration. As the basis of our state transition model we use the constant acceleration (CA) dynamical model [16] state tran-sition matrix. The CA model is applied to the reference Fig. 2. Length and width are treated as quasi-static in the motion model. In this motion model ψ is also treated as constant over one step, the changes in yaw are updated through the learned AM-KNet only. The observation model is defined by a linear measurement matrix H that selects the observed components from the full state vector. B. Dataset 

We train and evaluate AM-KNet on two automotive datasets, nuScenes [14] and View-of-Delft (VoD) [15]. VoD was recorded in urban traffic in Delft, the Netherlands, with synchronised frames captured at approximately 10 Hz using a lidar, a front camera, and a radar. Ground truth (GT) bounding box annotations are provided within the camera field of view. We used 36609 samples from 686 sequences as training data, 

32404 samples across 640 sequences as validation data, and 

18642 samples over 346 sequences as test data. The radar and lidar pointclouds were processed based on the Ground truth bounding boxes to emulate a detection algorithm. From the clusters we compute a centroid, the reference point, median cartesian velocity vector for radar, and a convex hull to obtain length and width of the measured bounding box. The nuScenes dataset was collected across Boston and Singapore, using a sensor suite consisting of one 360 ◦ li-dar, five radar units, and six surround-view cameras. Three-dimensional bounding-box annotations are provided at 2Hz for 23 object categories. We use 458 scenes for training, 153 for validation and 151 for testing. Due to lacking radar quality we use lidar and camera data in our experimental setup. We restrict our data to objects of the classes car, truck, bus, trailer, construction vehicle, pedestrian, motorcycle, bicycle .We use a total of 290694 timesteps across 14149 sequences for training, 96497 timesteps over 4664 sequences for validation and 99780 timesteps across 4810 sequences for testing, this is equal to a 60 /20 /20 split. As in VoD the lidar clusters are processed to extract the centroid and the reference point for each cluster. The camera images from nuScenes were given to a BevDepth [21] detector which output the detected 3D

bounding boxes. The detected bounding boxes were processed to extract a reference point. In Fig. 2 the error distribution for Lidar and Camera detections on the nuScenes dataset can be seen. 

C. Training 

Each training sample consists of a tracked object sequence of fixed legnth K = 100 time steps. Sequences are constructed from the respective dataset annotations and grouped into batches of B = 20 sequences. All computations are performed in double precision ( float64 ), however, it is completely feasi-ble to run this framework in single precision ( float16 ). The network is trained using the AdamW [22] optimizer. We use several regularisation mechanisms, dropout is applied to all layers and the weight decay of the Adam optimiser acts as L2

regularisation on the learnable parameters. Further the outputs of the GRU unitsare normalised using layer normalisation. Our training follows a three-phase schedule. During the first 

5 epochs of training the stationary target loss and the two Gaussian NLL terms are inactive, permitting the network to first learn accurate dynamics and state estimates. From epoch 

5 onward the loss term for stationary targets is enabled which encourages an inertia in the estimates of static targets. After epoch 8 the estimation error and innovation NLL’s are enabled. This staged introduction prevents the covariance heads from dominating the gradients before the state estimates have started converging. During training at each timestep k we extrapolate the state vector into the current timestep and call sequential update calls for each available sensor. The VoD data was synchronized, so we treat the measurements as originating from the same timestamp. In nuScenes the measurements are assigned to the same frame, but have not been synchronized exactly. For this case we predict once to the earliest sensor and perform the update. VII. D ISCUSSION 

A. Accuracy and adaptiveness 

Across both datasets the AM-KNet variants consistently achieve the lowest position and extent MAEs. The margin grows on nuScenes, which has a higher the diversity of target classes, ranges, and sensor configurations. While the baseline methods performance could be improved through intensive tuning to the data these results show that AM-KNet’s sensor-specific measurement modules and learned covariance branches allow it to adapt to the distinct characteristics of each modality, and the context modulation in AM-KNet+CM provides further conditioning on target type and motion state. This adaptiveness is especially visible in the velocity results on nuScenes, while UKF-EOT retains the advantage in vy on VoD. This indicating that the learned estimators do not yet fully capture lateral dynamics in the VoD setting. 

B. Consistency 

The NEES results reveal the most significant advantage of the learned approach. On VoD, AM-KNet achieves 77 .0% 

position NEES consistency. On nuScenes, AM-KNet+CM leads with 60 .3% . In contrast, the high NEES mean of the baseline methods indicates severe overconfidence. These extreme values suggest that fixed or heuristically adapted noise models degenerate when confronted with the variety of targets and sensors in the multi-target evaluation. OAFuser’s innovation-based adaptation is effective in con-trolled settings: it achieves the best position NIS position con-sistency and velocity NEES on VoD. However, this advantage does not transfer to position NEES or to nuScenes, where its consistency degrades sharply. AM-KNet’s data-driven adap-tation, by contrast, scales more robustly to heterogeneous conditions. VIII. C ONCLUSION 

We presented Adaptive Multi-modal KalmanNet (AM-KNet), a hybrid state estimator that preserves the recursive predict–update structure of the Kalman filter while replacing the covariance and gain computations with learned modules. Three architectural contributions address the limitations of the original KalmanNet on real-world automotive data. First, sensor-specific measurement modules enable the network to learn the distinct noise characteristics of radar, lidar, and camera independently, rather than treating all observations identically. Second, a covariance estimation branch based on the Joseph form, supervised through negative log-likelihood losses on both the estimation error and the innovation, provides an explicit and calibrated uncertainty output. Third, a hyper-network with context modulation conditions the filter on target type, motion state, and relative pose, allowing a single model to adapt to the diverse scenarios encountered in autonomous driving. The learned variants achieve superior estimation ac-curacy on position and extent states and, importantly, produce substantially more consistent covariance estimates than the rule-based baselines.
