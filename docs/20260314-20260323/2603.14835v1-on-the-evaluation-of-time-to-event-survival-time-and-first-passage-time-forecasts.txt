Abstract 

Time-to-event forecasts are essential when decisions depend on event timing. This article develops a framework for evaluating such forecasts when the event (a) has not yet occurred or (b) is not predicted within the forecast horizon. We introduce a theory of provisional evaluation, in which each forecast is assessed against its right-censored realization, defined as the minimum of the event time and the evaluation time. For probabilistic forecasts, we show that strictly proper scoring rules induce provisionally strictly proper scoring rules, whose expected score – computed from the right-censored realization – is optimized under truthful forecasting. Threshold-weighted versions of the continuous ranked probability score and the logarithmic score satisfy this property. We also develop a theory for scoring point (single-valued) forecasts under right-censoring. Quantile and interquartile range forecasts are shown to be provisionally elicitable, meaning that scoring functions exist for which these functionals uniquely minimize the expected score, whereas the expectation functional is not provisionally elicitable. A synthetic experiment demonstrates that the proposed scores correctly rank forecasters. Diagnostic tools, including Murphy diagrams and reliability diagrams, extend naturally. Applications to operational time-to-flood and time-to-strong-wind forecasts illustrate the approach. 

Keywords: Consistent scoring function; Decision theory; Elicitability; First passage time forecast; Fore-cast evaluation; Forecast ranking; Forecast verification; Proper scoring rule; Survival analysis; Time-to-event forecast. 

# 1 Introduction 

Time-to-event forecasts are critical in domains where the timing of an event — not merely its occurrence — has major implications for decision making, planning, and resource allocation. Such forecasts play important roles across diverse fields, including healthcare, engineering, emergency management, human resources, finance, and the physical and life sciences. Examples include survival time for cancer patients (Llobera et al., 2000), failure time for mechanical devices (Wang et al., 2015), timing of geo-mechanical failure and landslides (Mufundirwa et al., 2010), tsunami arrival time (Greenslade et al., 2019), flood peak arrival (Langridge et al., 2021), creep rupture time of polymers (Spathis & Kontou, 2012), customer churn (Huang et al., 2012), and time to loan default (Sarlija et al., 2009). Given their importance, such forecasts should be evaluated using rigorous methods to identify superior forecasters or models, promote continuous improvement, and ultimately support better decision making. While a robust suite of evaluation techniques and diagnostic tools has emerged over the past two decades (e.g., Gneiting & Raftery, 2007; Gneiting, 2011a; Ehm et al., 2016; Dimitriadis et al., 2024), two challenges commonly arise in the evaluation of time-to-event forecasts, requiring extensions of existing tools. First, at evaluation time, some cases lack realized event times because the event has not yet occurred. Second, the forecasting model may have a finite prediction horizon, and the event lies beyond that horizon for some cases. This paper develops evaluation methods to address both challenges — or explains why such methods cannot exist in some situations. 1

We consider two main forms of time-to-event forecasts: (i) predictive distributions, which specify the likelihood of event timing over all possible future times, and (ii) single-value (‘24 months’) or interval (‘20 to 30 months’) forecasts, often representing summary statistics such as the mean or interquartile range (IQR) of a predictive distribution. Predictive distributions provide the most comprehensive representation of uncertainty in event timing. They are common in survival analysis, often expressed as survival functions and generated using statistical techniques (Kaplan & Meier, 1958; Cox, 1972; Wang et al., 2019). Ensemble prediction systems in weather and hydrology (Mureau et al., 1993; Molteni et al., 1996; Schaake et al., 2007; Cloke & Pappenberger, 2009) have enabled the increased generation of probabilistic forecasts, occasionally including time-to-event predictions (Roksv˚ ag et al., 2023). Probabilistic first-passage-time forecasts also appear in physics, biology, and finance (Weiss, 1967; Patie, 2004; Iyer-Biswas & Zilman, 2016). To assess and compare the accuracy of different predictive methods, we evaluate the forecasts they gen-erate against the corresponding observed outcomes. Scoring rules provide summary measures by assigning a numerical score S(F, t ) to a predictive distribution F when compared with the corresponding realized event time t. A well-developed theory of scoring rules has emerged over the past two decades (Gneiting & Raftery, 2007; Gneiting & Katzfuss, 2014), advocating the use of proper scoring rules . Proper scoring rules incentivize honest forecasts based on careful probabilistic assessment and reward predictive distributions that are sharp yet calibrated (Gneiting & Raftery, 2007). They enable the ranking of forecasters by mean score over a com-mon set of cases. When a scoring rule is not proper, it may assign better scores to inferior forecasts, leading to misguided inferences. The theory of proper scoring rules assumes evaluation against the realized event time t. However, at evaluation time τ , the event may not yet have occurred, in which case t is unknown and S(F, t ) cannot be computed. The survival analysis literature addresses this by replacing the unknown t with the known right-censored realization min( t, τ ) and by modifying the scoring rule accordingly. We call this approach provisional evaluation and develop a corresponding theory of scoring rules for this setting. A scoring rule is provisionally strictly proper on the time interval [0 , τ ] if the only way a forecaster can optimize their expected score under evaluation against min( t, τ ) is to issue a predictive distribution that aligns with their true probabilistic as-sessment on [0 , τ ) (Definition 4). We show that provisionally strictly proper scoring rules can be constructed from strictly proper scoring rules for predictive distributions (Theorem 1) and predictive densities (Theorem 2; cf. Holzmann & Klar 2017). Consequently, we establish that threshold-weighted versions of the continuous ranked probability score (CRPS) (Gneiting & Ranjan, 2011) and logarithmic score (Diks et al., 2011) are pro-visionally strictly proper (Corollaries 1 and 2). This builds on related work by Rindt et al. (2022), who show that the threshold-weighted logarithmic score is provisionally proper, while some other scores used in survival analysis — such as the integrated Brier score (Graf et al., 1999) and survival-CRPS (Avati et al., 2020) — are not. Measures of discrimination such as the concordance index (Harrell Jr et al., 1996) are also not proper (Blanche et al., 2019; Rindt et al., 2022). We illustrate the theory using well-designed synthetic experiments that confirm that (provisionally) strictly proper scoring rules rank forecasters correctly, while improper rules can mislead. We extend this framework to single-valued and interval forecasts. To guide forecasters in issuing such predictions, Murphy & Daan (1985), Engelberg et al. (2009), and Gneiting (2011a) argue that either the scoring function should be specified in advance or a specific functional of the predictive distribution (e.g., mean, median, or 90th percentile) should be requested. A scoring function is strictly consistent for a functional if the only way a forecaster can optimize their expected score is to report the true functional value of their predictive distribution (Gneiting, 2011a). For example, squared error is strictly consistent for the expectation (i.e., mean), and absolute error for the median. A functional is elicitable if it admits a strictly consistent scoring function (Lambert et al., 2008). The mean, median, and quantiles are elicitable (Savage, 1971; Thomson, 1978; Saerens, 2000), whereas the mode and conditional value-at-risk are not (Heinrich, 2014; Gneiting, 2011a). For provisional evaluation, we say that a scoring function is provisionally strictly consistent for the func-tional on the time interval [0 , τ ] if the only way a forecaster can optimize their expected score, given evaluation against the right-censored realization min( t, τ ), is to report the functional of their predictive distribution, ‘mod-ulo right-censorship’ (Definition 5). A functional is provisionally elicitable if it admits a provisionally strictly consistent scoring function (Definition 6). Quantile functionals are provisionally elicitable (Theorem 3) via threshold-weighted variants of quantile loss (Taggart, 2022a). Predictive intervals such as the IQR, where endpoints are quantiles, are also elicitable (Theorem 4) via threshold-weighted variants of the classical interval score (Dunsmore, 1968; Winkler, 1972), which we introduce in this paper. Synthetic experiments confirm that (provisionally) strictly consistent scoring functions rank forecasters correctly, while inconsistent scoring functions can mislead. We also give general conditions under which a functional is not provisionally elicitable 2(Theorem 5), and use these to show that the expectation functional is not provisionally elicitable (Corol-lary 3). This imposes serious limitations on the evaluation of expected time-to-event forecasts in situations where events may take years to materialize. Recall the second challenge for time-to-event evaluation: events may not occur within the model’s prediction horizon. This challenge arises for infrequent meteorological events when using numerical weather prediction models, whose prediction horizons are typically less than 15 days. The threshold-weighted scores addressing the first challenge are invariant to right-censoring of forecasts, permitting coherent evaluation of predictive distributions, quantile forecasts, and predictive intervals censored to the model’s prediction horizon. No such solution exists for expected time-to-event forecasts. To complement ranking methods based on provisionally strictly proper and consistent scores, we show that several other important diagnostic tools apply to censored data. Brier score decompositions (Schumacher et al., 2003) and Murphy diagrams (Ehm et al., 2016) provide graphical diagnostics of performance across decision thresholds, and can reveal cases where one method outperforms another for decision making at different time scales despite similar mean scores. Reliability diagrams for quantile forecasts, derived via isotonic regression (cf. Barlow et al., 1972; Dimitriadis et al., 2021; Jordan et al., 2022), expose conditional biases and enable optimal in-sample recalibration under monotonicity constraints, even with right-censoring. The remainder of the article is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews proper scoring rules and consistent scoring functions for uncensored data and illustrates them using a synthetic experiment. Section 3 adapts this theory for right-censored realizations and forecasts, again illustrated with synthetic data with the aid of Murphy diagrams. Section 4 applies the theory to operational time-to-flood and time-to-strong-wind forecasts from the Australian Bureau of Meteorology, with particular emphasis on reliability diagrams and recalibration. Section 5 concludes with a discussion of extensions to left- and interval-censoring. Proofs of new results appear in the appendix. 

# 2 Evaluation of time-to-event forecasts when both forecasts and realizations are known 

This section reviews established evaluation methods for time-to-event forecasts when both forecasts and real-izations are known. We focus on the key concepts of strictly proper scoring rules, strictly consistent scoring functions, and elicitability, as these form the foundation for the modifications introduced later to handle right-censored data. To illustrate these concepts – and new concepts introduced later – we also present a synthetic experiment for time-to-event forecasts. The general mathematical set-up and notation is as follows. The set of possible times at which a well-defined event could occur will be represented by an interval I of the real line taking the form [0 , ∞), [0 , b ) or [0 , b ] for some positive b. The interior of I consists of I excluding its endpoints. The time-to-event random variable will typically be denoted by T and takes values in I, and its realization (or observation) will typically be denoted by t. (For now we do not consider the case where there is positive probability that the event never materializes, though an extension of the theory to this case is discussed in Section 3.3.) We adopt a measure-theoretic framework for probability distributions and probability density functions (PDFs). To make the body of this paper as accessible as possible, technical terms related to measure theory are restricted to this paragraph and the appendix. Suffice to say, the setting described below is very general and includes virtually every probability distribution and PDF that arises in practical applications. Throughout, a time-to-event predictive distribution (i.e. probabilistic forecast) F is assumed to be a Borel probability measure on I, and a class F of time-to-event predictive distributions is assumed to be contained within the set of Borel probability measures on I. A predictive distribution F will be identified with its cumulative distribution function (CDF), such that given any time s in I, F (s) is the forecast probability that T does not exceed s. Throughout we tacitly assume that scoring functions S and associated weight functions w are Borel measurable. Occasionally we work with classes F of probability measures that have densities (i.e. PDFs); in this case each probability measure in F is absolutely continuous with respect to the Lebesgue measure. 

# 2.1 Proper scoring rules 

A scoring rule assigns a numerical score S(F, t ) to each pair ( F, t ), where F is a time-to-event predictive distribution and t is the time-to-event realization. In this paper we adopt the convention that scoring rules are negatively oriented, so that the score represents a penalty and a lower score is better. 3Given a random variable T and probability distributions F and G, we write EF S(G, T ) to denote the expectation of the score S(G, T ) given that the random variable T is distributed according to F , assuming tacitly that the expectation exists. 

Definition 1. Suppose that F is a class of probability distributions on I. A scoring rule S : F × I → (−∞ , ∞]is said to be proper relative to the class F if 

EF S(F, T ) ≤ EF S(G, T ) (1) for all F and G in F. It is strictly proper if equality in (1) implies that F = G.Proper scoring rules offer several advantages. If a time-to-event process T follows distribution F , then F

achieves the best possible expected score under a proper scoring rule, and uniquely so under a strictly proper scoring rule. Moreover, if forecaster A makes optimal use of their available information and forecaster B has access to additional information and uses it optimally, then forecaster B will attain a better expected score when the scoring rule is strictly proper (Holzmann & Eulert, 2014). Finally, if a forecaster genuinely believes the time-to-event process has distribution F , then issuing F optimizes their expected score under a proper scoring rule. Thus, score optimization aligns with honest forecasting, avoiding the forecaster’s dilemma (Lerch et al., 2017). # 2.3 Consistent scoring functions 

While a predictive distribution F for a time-to-event variable T provides the most complete representation of uncertainty, in many practical settings a summary – such as the mean, a specific percentile, or the interquartile range (IQR) – offers greater utility for decision making, communication, or reporting. For example, when informing a cancer patient of their predicted survival time, the phrase “6 to 9 months” is far more accessible than a graph of a survival function. Mappings that present some summary feature of a distribution are called 

functionals . Table 3 provides examples of functionals for the predictive distributions of Figure 1. In the context of time-to-event forecasts, a functional U is a map from some class F of probability distributions on the interval I to a subset of Ik for some k ≥ 1. In many practical contexts, typically k = 1 and U (F ) is a singleton (i.e. U (F ) is a single value) from I, such as when U is the mean (i.e. expectation) functional. But sometimes U (F ) may have multiple values, such as when U is the median functional and the graph of the predictive CDF F is horizontal at height 0 .5. # 3.3 Dealing with right-censored forecasts 

Sections 3.1 and 3.2 addressed forecast evaluation using right-censored realizations. We now consider evaluation of right-censored time-to-event forecasts. Such forecasts arise naturally in numerical weather prediction (NWP) models, which provide atmospheric and surface forecasts with horizons typically less than 15 days. If the event is the first time, from the model ‘initial condition time’, that a typhoon appears in the South China Sea then frequently the event will not be forecast within the prediction horizon τ of the model. In such cases, one can say that the time-to-event forecast x is greater than τ , so that [ x]τ is known even if the model does not produce a value for x.Fortunately, the provisionally strictly proper scoring rules and provisionally strictly consistent scoring functions of Sections 3.1 and 3.2 are invariant with respect to right-censorship of the forecast. That is, the threshold-weighted CRPS (15, 18) and threshold-weighted logarithmic score (23, 24) can be calculated if the values F (s) of the predictive distribution are only known for 0 ≤ s < τ , so that [ F ]τ is known even if 

F is unknown. Similarly, the values of the provisionally strictly consistent scores (26, 27, 28) for quantile and interval forecasts are unchanged if the forecast x is replaced with [ x]τ . The CRPS decomposition and Murphy diagram diagnostic tools illustrated in Figure 2 are also invariant with respect to right-censorship of the forecast, provided that one restricts the diagrams to decision thresholds from 0 to τ .In summary, right-censored CDF, quantile and interval forecasts can be evaluated using the threshold-weighted scores introduced earlier. As illustrated in Section 4, such forecasts can also be derived from ensembles of time-series forecasts with finite prediction horizons. However, right-censored mean (expected value) forecasts cannot be derived from an ensemble if the event fails to occur in any member within the model’s prediction horizon. Right-censorship of forecasts at time τ can also be used in situations where the predicted probability that the event never occurs is positive, so that the outcome space is the extended half real line [0 , ∞] and 

P(T = ∞) > 0, where T = ∞ denotes the possibility that the event never occurs. In such cases, a predictive distribution F , defined by F (s) = P(T ≤ s) whenever s ∈ [0 , ∞], has right-censored counterparts [ F ]τ that can be used for provisional evaluation as discussed above. Finally, note that while only the values of right-censored forecasts are required for these evaluation methods, it is useful for communication to indicate whether a forecast has been censored. For example, if the censoring time is 12 months and the right-censored median forecast equals 12 months, one could state: 

• “The median survival time is 12 months” if the median is known to be exactly 12 months; 

• “The median survival time is at least 12 months” if the true median is unknown; 

• “The median survival time is greater than 12 months” if the true median is unknown but known not to equal the censoring time. 

# 4 Examples from weather and hydrological prediction 

# 4.1 First passage time forecasts for flood 

Forecasting the first time a river exceeds a critical flood level, relative to model initiation, is an example of a first passage time forecast. # 4.2 Diagnostic for conditional biases and the calibration time-to-event quantile forecasts: an example using wind speed forecasts 

This example illustrates the use of isotonic regression as a diagnostic for conditional biases in time-to-event forecasts and as a tool for forecast calibration. Both forecasts and realizations are right-censored. Consider a forecast service designed for small recreational boats and windsurfers in Botany Bay, Sydney, Australia. Wind speeds above 15 knots (7 .7 ms −1) are unsafe for small boats, whereas windsurfers typically require winds of at least this strength. The service provides forecasts of the first time that wind speed exceeds 15 knots during an 18-hour validity period (18:00-12:00 UTC), which spans daylight and twilight conditions. Forecasts are issued daily at 06:00 UTC – 12 hours before the validity period – and expressed as an interquartile range (IQR). Verification is performed using observations from the Kurnell weather station on Botany Bay. We use hourly wind speed forecasts from the high-resolution ACCESS model for the Sydney domain (Rennie et al., 2022), initialized daily at 00:00 UTC with a 36-hour horizon, and archived by the Jive verification system (Loveday et al., 2024). Figure 4 shows forecasts and observations for the validity period 3 January 2023 18:00 UTC to 4 January 2023 12:00 UTC. The realized time-to-event is 5.35 hours after the start of the validity period; the forecast time-to-event is 15.35 hours, obtained via linear interpolation. As an initial assessment, we derive single-valued time-to-event forecasts x via linear interpolation from hourly ACCESS forecasts and interpret them as deterministic distributions, forming IQR forecasts ( x, x ). Each validity period is 18 hours, so τ = 18, and scoring using twIS 0.5,18 (28) reduces to the absolute error between the right-censored forecast and realization. Using 2023 data, the mean score twIS 0.5,18 is 6.56 hours – over one-third of the validity duration – indicating substantial room for improvement. Apply these bias corrections to each hourly forecast. If the threshold is not exceeded, assign a dummy value greater than 18 to the time-to-event forecast; we use 1000. As discussed later, the choice of dummy value is immaterial.

Time-to-event quantile forecasts for finite validity periods τ can be evalu-ated using threshold-weighted quantile scores, which depend only on right-censored forecasts and realizations, making the choice of dummy value irrelevant given it exceeds τ . Moreover, Jordan et al. (2022) show that the min–max solution to isotonic regression for quantiles is optimal for any consistent scoring function for quantiles, including threshold-weighted scores. This extends naturally to interval forecasts, assessed via the threshold-weighted interval score (a sum of two quantile scores). Consequently, the circle markers in Figure 5 within the region [0 , τ ) × [0 , τ ) are unaffected by the choice of dummy value. Choosing a dummy value greater than τ rather than equal to τ clarifies whether a quantile exceeds the validity period, aiding interpretation. For example, if the recalibrated 0.75-quantile exceeds τ , one can state: “the 75th percentile for the event time lies beyond the forecast validity period.” 

# 5 Discussion 

Evaluating time-to-event forecasts is challenging when realizations are unknown because the event has not materialized, or forecasts are incomplete because the event did not occur (or occur with certainty) within the prediction horizon. To address these challenges, we proposed evaluation methods that combine right-censoring concepts from survival analysis with proper scoring rules and consistent scoring functions from mathematical statistics. Specifically, we advocate provisional evaluation using provisionally strictly proper scoring rules 19 Figure 5: Reliability curves from isotonic regression using Botany Bay data: (a) 2023 time-to-event forecasts derived from bias-corrected wind speed forecasts, (b) 2024 uncalibrated and calibrated time-to-event quantile forecasts. (e.g., threshold-weighted CRPS and logarithmic score) and provisionally strictly consistent scoring functions (e.g., threshold-weighted quantile and interval scores). These scores avoid misleading inferences about forecast quality under right-censoring, as illustrated by the synthetic example. Importantly, Theorems 1 and 2 open up the possibility of constructing broad classes of new provisionally strictly proper scoring rules, induced from existing strictly proper scoring rules. While threshold-weighted logarithmic scores have previously been applied to survival data (Rindt et al., 2022), the use of threshold-weighted CRPS, quantile loss, and interval scores for this purpose appears novel. The threshold-weighted CRPS is especially well suited to ensemble-based forecast distributions, as frequently encountered in meteorological and hydrological applications, because it admits a computationally efficient closed form and naturally accommodates distributional discontinuities. The threshold-weighted CRPS avoids the impropriety issues associated with survival-CRPS (Rindt et al., 2022; Avati et al., 2020), and our approach to scoring quantile forecasts is substantially less restrictive than a recent attempt to render Portnoy’s estimator proper (Yanagisawa, 2023; Portnoy, 2003). Conversely, we showed that the mean (expectation) functional is not provisionally elicitable and provided sufficient conditions for identifying other functionals that share this fundamental limitation. Threshold-weighted CRPS and quantile scores also admit decompositions into Brier and elementary scores, enabling graphical diagnostics for interpreting provisional rankings through a decision-theoretic lens, even under right-censoring. Although not illustrated here, a similar diagnostic exists for the threshold-weighted 20 interval score, since it is the sum of two quantile scores. We are unaware of an analogous decomposition for the threshold-weighted logarithmic score. This paper also introduced reliability diagrams for quantile fore-casts under right-censoring using isotonic regression, which supports CORP-style decompositions (Dimitriadis et al., 2021, 2024) into uncertainty, miscalibration, and discrimination components. It is likely that isotonic distributional regression (Henzi et al., 2021) could similarly be applied to time-to-event predictive distributions. Our results show that predictive distributions, quantiles, and intervals for time-to-event forecasts can be evaluated soundly under right-censoring. However, the provisional non-elicitability of the expectation functional implies that single-valued forecasts targeting the mean cannot be scored soundly using right-censored data. This may be inconsequential for short-term events. However, it is problematic for events that may take years to occur and questions the practice of training models on squared loss in such cases. While long-term expected time-to-event forecasts may remain useful for some applications, their quality must be inferred from proxies such as provisional evaluation of predictive distributions, which cannot fully assess tail behavior and thus cannot guarantee mean-value accuracy. Interestingly, threshold-weighted scores were originally motivated by the need to evaluate predictive per-formance with emphasis on extreme events (Diks et al., 2011; Gneiting & Ranjan, 2011; Taggart, 2022a; Allen et al., 2023). Here, we identify another important role: handling right-censored data. The use of left-censored and interval-censored data is of interest in the survival literature (Lindsey & Ryan, 1998; Gomez et al., 1992) and elsewhere. Although not explored in this paper, we expect that extensions of our results to left- and interval-censoring are feasible by adapting threshold weights to the specific censoring structure.
